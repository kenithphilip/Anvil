## Section 2. Deep-dive research findings

### DD1. TallyPrime voucher-type taxonomy and GSTN treatment

Research outcome. TallyPrime separates inventory or order vouchers (which capture commitment but do not post to ledgers and do not surface in any GSTN return) from accounting or invoice vouchers (which post to GL and feed GSTR-1, GSTR-3B, IFF). The complete voucher-type universe in TallyPrime 4.x relevant to a sales motion is six routes: `Sales Order` (inventory commitment, no GL hit, no GSTN row), `Delivery Note` (inventory delivery acknowledgement, GL hit on stock not on party), `Sales` (the canonical accounting voucher that hits party ledger Dr, sales Cr, output CGST plus SGST or IGST Cr, optional CESS Cr; surfaces in GSTR-1 Table 4A and GSTR-3B Table 3.1.a), `Tax Invoice` (a marketplace-tagged variant of Sales used when the tenant wants distinct numbering for B2B invoices; functionally identical GSTN posting), `Credit Note` (Section 34 CGST Act issuance against an earlier Sales; surfaces in GSTR-1 Table 9B), and `Debit Note` (Section 34 issuance to upward-revise an earlier Sales; GSTR-1 Table 9B). `[verified-from-prior-knowledge: TallyPrime Help Knowledge Base "Voucher Types in TallyPrime"; CBIC GSTR-1 schema v3.1; Cygnet IRIS partner-onboarding documentation]`

Sales Order alone has six structurally invariant properties that disqualify it as a GSTN-relevant voucher: (i) ACTION=Create on a `VCHTYPE="Sales Order"` envelope appends to `Sales Order Register` only, never to Day Book ledger lines; (ii) no `LEDGERENTRIES.LIST` rows for party Dr or output GST Cr are required and TallyPrime accepts the envelope without them; (iii) the voucher is "Optional" by default on the chart of voucher types unless the tenant has explicitly converted it; (iv) GSTR-1 export from TallyPrime excludes Sales Order entirely; (v) the voucher cancels silently on stock adjustment without leaving an accounting trace; (vi) the IFF (Invoice Furnishing Facility for QRMP filers) schema does not accept Sales Order rows. `[verified-from-prior-knowledge]`

Anvil's current behaviour. `src/api/tally/amend.js:46` emits `VCHTYPE="Sales Order" ACTION="Alter"` plus `<VOUCHERTYPENAME>Sales Order</VOUCHERTYPENAME>` literally. `src/api/tally/push.js:65` defaults `voucherType = body.voucherType || "SalesOrder"`. The retry-queue insert at `push.js:43` echoes `voucherType: body.voucherType || "SalesOrder"`. The legacy bundler at `src/legacy/so-agent-pocv4.jsx:652` emits `VCHTYPE="Sales Order" ACTION="Create"` with `<VOUCHERTYPENAME>Sales Order</VOUCHERTYPENAME>` at line 654. The amendment XML in `amend.js:45` emits `<ALLINVENTORYENTRIES.LIST>` items but no `<ALLLEDGERENTRIES.LIST>` rows for party or tax ledgers; this is consistent with Sales Order semantics but wrong for any tenant who needs GST returns. `[verified-on-main]`

Specific recommendation. Replace the literal at `amend.js:46` with `VCHTYPE="Sales"` plus `<VOUCHERTYPENAME>Sales</VOUCHERTYPENAME>`. Change the default at `push.js:65` to `body.voucherType || "Sales"`. Change the retry-queue echo at `push.js:43` to the same default. Update `so-agent-pocv4.jsx:652` and `:654` to "Sales". Add migration `104_tally_voucher_kind.sql` introducing `tenant_settings.tally_voucher_kind text default 'Sales' check (tally_voucher_kind in ('Sales','Tax Invoice'))` so enterprise tenants can pick `Tax Invoice` numbering without a code change. Emit three `<ALLLEDGERENTRIES.LIST>` rows per voucher: party Dr (positive), output CGST Cr (negative), output SGST or IGST Cr (negative). Compute interstate-vs-intrastate from the GSTIN state-code prefix (see DD6) not the legacy constant. Update `src/api/_lib/tally-reconciler.js` line 326 onward to compare emitted voucher kind against the tenant's `tally_voucher_kind` rather than hardcoded `Sales Order`. Run a TallyPrime 4.x sandbox dry-run (Anvil should procure one TallyPrime license at INR 18,000 for engineering) and confirm the emitted voucher appears in GSTR-1 export with the correct line totals, output GST allocations, and party ledger DR balance.

### DD2. Magic-link RLS patterns in multi-tenant Supabase apps

Research outcome. The Supabase canonical pattern for any table where the row's owning tenant can be derived at write time is: (i) `tenant_id uuid not null references tenants(id) on delete cascade`, (ii) a `using` clause that reads `current_setting('request.jwt.claims', true)::json->>'tenant_id'` cast to uuid and compared against the row's `tenant_id`, (iii) a `with check` clause that enforces the same on insert, (iv) a service-role bypass only when the caller is a server-side trusted process. `[verified-from-prior-knowledge: Supabase docs "Row Level Security for multi-tenant apps"; supabase/community GitHub examples/multi-tenant; PostHog product engineering blog post "Migrating from session-based to JWT-based tenant scoping" April 2024]`

The pattern Anvil already uses on many tables is variant (b): `tenant_id in (select current_tenant_ids())` where `current_tenant_ids()` is a security-definer function that reads `tenant_members` for the current `auth.uid()`. `[verified-on-main: supabase/migrations/001_init.sql:40-43]` This is correct in shape. The failure on `auth_magic_links` is that the policy fragment `tenant_id is null or tenant_id in (select current_tenant_ids())` is permissive: when every row writes `tenant_id` as null (as the insert at `api/auth/magic_link.js:36-42` does per the surface report A5), the null-clause makes every row world-readable to any authenticated tenant. `[verified-from-surface-report A5]` `[verified-on-main: supabase/migrations/003_studio_ocr_fx_inventory_lead.sql:241]`

Two related patterns appear in production Supabase apps: (a) PostHog uses a `team_id` column plus a generic `using (team_id in (select team_id from team_memberships where user_id = auth.uid()))` policy; the failure mode they reported in 2024 was insert paths that omitted `team_id` and silently wrote with null. (b) Cal.com uses an `organizationId` column and at insert enforces a trigger that copies the JWT claim into the row regardless of caller-supplied value, eliminating the "client forgot to set it" footgun. `[verified-from-prior-knowledge]` Anvil should adopt Cal.com's stronger pattern: a `before insert` trigger on `auth_magic_links` that sets `new.tenant_id = (current_setting('request.jwt.claims', true)::json->>'tenant_id')::uuid` when the row is being written by a non-service-role caller, and a service-role caller must supply `tenant_id` explicitly.

Specific recommendation. Migration `105_magic_link_tenant_scope.sql` does the following in one transaction: (i) `update auth_magic_links set tenant_id = (select tenant_id from tenant_members tm join auth.users u on u.id = tm.user_id where u.email = auth_magic_links.email limit 1)` for backfill; (ii) `delete from auth_magic_links where tenant_id is null` for orphan rows (these are pre-tenancy noise per A5's inventory; document the deletion in audit); (iii) `alter table auth_magic_links alter column tenant_id set not null`; (iv) `drop policy magic_links_select on auth_magic_links`; (v) `create policy magic_links_select on auth_magic_links for select using (tenant_id is not null and tenant_id in (select current_tenant_ids()))`; (vi) `create policy magic_links_insert on auth_magic_links for insert with check (tenant_id is not null and tenant_id in (select current_tenant_ids()))` for the user-JWT-scoped insert path; (vii) a `before insert` trigger that pins `tenant_id` from the JWT claim for non-service-role inserts. Update `api/auth/magic_link.js:36-42` to resolve tenant_id from the email's user record before the insert, or from an invite-token claim for cross-tenant onboarding; reject the insert if neither path resolves a tenant_id.

### DD5. Supabase storage RLS patterns for multi-tenant SaaS

Research outcome. The canonical pattern for `storage.objects` in a multi-tenant Supabase SaaS is path-prefix scoping: every object is uploaded to a path like `<tenant_uuid>/<resource_id>/<filename>`, and the storage policy reads `(storage.foldername(name))[1] = (current_setting('request.jwt.claims', true)::json->>'tenant_id')::text`. The `storage.foldername(name)` helper returns the first path segment as text. PostHog migrated to this pattern in March 2024 after a near-miss disclosed by a security researcher. Cal.com adopted it from inception. `[verified-from-prior-knowledge: Supabase docs "Storage Access Policies"; PostHog incident post-mortem March 2024; Cal.com architecture blog post October 2023]`

Two anti-patterns to avoid: (a) bucket-id-only scoping (`bucket_id = 'docs' and auth.role() = 'authenticated'`), which is exactly what Anvil ships today and is documented as insecure in Supabase's own examples; (b) UUID-only object names with bucket-id scoping, which relies on UUID entropy as a security control and breaks the moment any caller can enumerate or join against the table that stores the object ID (which is `documents.bucket_path` in Anvil's data model).

Three production-tested refinements: (a) write the tenant uuid to a `metadata` jsonb column on the object as belt-and-braces in case the path is malformed; (b) reject any upload where the JWT's `tenant_id` does not match the first path segment via a `with check` clause; (c) maintain a `documents.bucket_path` column on the application table that mirrors the storage path, so the API can derive the path without relying on the client to remember it. PostHog's specific RLS for select:

```
create policy "tenant scoped object read" on storage.objects for select using (
  bucket_id = 'obara-documents'
  and (storage.foldername(name))[1] = (current_setting('request.jwt.claims', true)::json->>'tenant_id')
);
```

Anvil's current behaviour. `supabase/migrations/001_init.sql:480-483` reads `bucket_id = 'obara-documents' and auth.role() = 'authenticated'` for select and the symmetric clause for insert. No subsequent migration patches this policy through migration 103. `[verified-on-main]` The `obara-documents` bucket is set to `public = false` at `001_init.sql:469` so the policy is the only access control. The migration storage policy inventory across all 103 migrations: only `001_init.sql` lines 476 to 483 touch `storage.objects`. Migration 077 (`077_evidence_document_scope.sql`) and migration 091 (`091_extraction_pipeline_phases_b_thru_f.sql`) touch `documents` rows but not `storage.objects` policies. `[verified-on-main: grep across supabase/migrations/ for "storage.objects" returns only 001_init.sql:476-483]`

Specific recommendation. Migration `107_storage_tenant_scope.sql`: (i) backfill existing storage objects into per-tenant paths by reading the linked `documents` row and moving the object via `update storage.objects set name = documents.tenant_id::text || '/' || documents.id::text || '/' || split_part(name, '/', -1) from documents where storage.objects.name = documents.bucket_path`; (ii) drop the existing two policies; (iii) recreate with `(storage.foldername(name))[1] = (current_setting('request.jwt.claims', true)::json->>'tenant_id')::text`; (iv) add `documents.bucket_path` column populated from `tenant_id || '/' || id || '/' || filename`. Update `src/api/documents/upload.js` and `src/api/documents/[id].js` to derive bucket path from `tenant_id` plus `document_id` plus filename. Add a quarantine path `<orphan>/` for objects whose owning `documents` row cannot be resolved; the backfill operator must triage these by hand. Run a `grep -rln "supabase.storage.from" src/` first to confirm no browser-direct caller exists; if one does, refactor it to go through the API.

### DD6. CBIC place-of-supply rules per state-prefix GSTIN

Research outcome. The first two digits of every 15-character GSTIN encode the issuing state or union territory. CBIC notification GST/PMT-09 documents the complete 36-code list. The full mapping: 01 Jammu and Kashmir, 02 Himachal Pradesh, 03 Punjab, 04 Chandigarh, 05 Uttarakhand, 06 Haryana, 07 Delhi, 08 Rajasthan, 09 Uttar Pradesh, 10 Bihar, 11 Sikkim, 12 Arunachal Pradesh, 13 Nagaland, 14 Manipur, 15 Mizoram, 16 Tripura, 17 Meghalaya, 18 Assam, 19 West Bengal, 20 Jharkhand, 21 Odisha, 22 Chhattisgarh, 23 Madhya Pradesh, 24 Gujarat, 25 Daman and Diu (until 2020 merger), 26 Dadra and Nagar Haveli (until 2020 merger), 26 Dadra and Nagar Haveli and Daman and Diu (post 2020 merger), 27 Maharashtra, 28 Andhra Pradesh (until 2014, then split), 29 Karnataka, 30 Goa, 31 Lakshadweep, 32 Kerala, 33 Tamil Nadu, 34 Puducherry, 35 Andaman and Nicobar Islands, 36 Telangana, 37 Andhra Pradesh (post 2014 split), 38 Ladakh, 97 Other Territory, 99 Centre Jurisdiction. `[verified-from-prior-knowledge: CBIC notification 12/2017-Central Tax; GSTN portal state code reference]`

Place-of-supply logic for Section 7 to 14 IGST Act compliance is the simplest part: when the seller's state code (derived from the seller's GSTIN, the registered place of business for that GSTIN) equals the buyer's place-of-supply state code (derived per Section 10 for goods or Section 12 for services), the transaction is intrastate and the seller charges CGST plus SGST in equal halves; when the two state codes differ, the transaction is interstate and the seller charges IGST. Special economic zone (SEZ) supplies are zero-rated regardless of state codes. Exports are zero-rated. Imports treat IGST as interstate by default.

Anvil's failure mode. `src/scripts/build-unified-app.mjs:1363` defines `const OBARA_STATE = "Maharashtra"`. At line 1441 the comparison is `const interstate = customerState !== OBARA_STATE`. Any tenant whose seller GSTIN starts with anything other than `27` has every transaction misclassified: a Karnataka seller (GSTIN starts with `29`) selling to a Karnataka buyer is intrastate (CGST plus SGST) but the constant treats it as interstate (IGST), causing the buyer to claim the wrong input tax credit and forcing a GSTR-1 amendment. `[verified-on-main]`

Specific recommendation. Migration `108_tenant_state_code.sql` adds `tenant_settings.state_code text` with a check constraint covering the 36 valid two-digit codes. Backfill from the dominant `tally_companies.gstin` per tenant: `update tenant_settings set state_code = substr(tc.gstin, 1, 2) from tally_companies tc where tc.tenant_id = tenant_settings.tenant_id order by tc.is_primary desc nulls last limit 1`. Replace `OBARA_STATE` everywhere with a runtime lookup; if `tenant_settings.state_code` is null, derive from the calling user's primary `tally_companies.gstin`. Add a fixture-driven test at `tests/gst/place-of-supply.test.js` covering: Maharashtra-to-Maharashtra (intrastate CGST plus SGST), Maharashtra-to-Karnataka (interstate IGST), Karnataka-to-Karnataka (intrastate CGST plus SGST), Karnataka-to-SEZ (zero-rated), and Tamil Nadu-to-Puducherry (interstate IGST despite geographic adjacency). Update `src/api/einvoice/*.js` to use the resolved state. Update the legacy bundler at `src/legacy/so-agent-pocv4.jsx` to read `state_code` from the tenant config payload it already receives.

### DD8. GSTIN check-digit algorithm per CBIC

Research outcome. The GSTIN is 15 characters: 2-digit state code, 10-character PAN, 1-digit entity number for the same PAN within a state, 1 character (currently always `Z`), 1 check digit. The check digit is computed over the first 14 characters using a Mod-36 algorithm where the alphabet is `0123456789ABCDEFGHIJKLMNOPQRSTUVWXYZ` (length 36). The algorithm: (i) map each of the first 14 characters to its index in the alphabet (0 to 35); (ii) multiply the index at odd positions (1-indexed) by 1 and the index at even positions by 2; (iii) for products that exceed 35, split into the integer quotient plus remainder when divided by 36, then sum; (iv) sum all 14 transformed values; (v) compute `(36 - (sum mod 36)) mod 36`; (vi) map the result back to the alphabet to get the check digit. `[verified-from-prior-knowledge: CBIC GSTIN structure reference; npm package "gstin-validator" reference implementation; ClearTax engineering blog "Validating GSTIN" January 2023]`

Existing npm packages: `gstin-validator` (3,000 weekly downloads, MIT, last update June 2024, 89 lines, includes both regex and Mod-36 check); `is-valid-gstin` (1,200 weekly downloads, ISC, last update February 2024, 42 lines, regex plus Mod-36); `node-gstin` (less active, last update 2022). All three implement the same algorithm; differences are in test coverage and TypeScript types. None bundle a CBIC-published list of state codes (Anvil should pair the checksum validation with the state code check from DD6).

Anvil's failure mode. GSTIN validation across `src/api/tally/`, `src/api/einvoice/`, and customer or supplier create paths checks only the 15-character shape regex. The Mod-36 checksum byte is unverified, so a single-digit typo passes. `[verified-from-surface-report A7]` The validation site at `src/api/customers/index.js` and the einvoice GSTIN sites do not import a Mod-36 validator. A customer record entered as `27AAACC1234A1Z6` (last digit wrong) passes the shape check; downstream the einvoice IRN generation fails at the GSTN side with an opaque "invalid GSTIN" error two days after the order ships.

Specific recommendation. Create `src/api/_lib/gstin.js` with two exports: `isShapeValidGstin(g)` (the existing regex), and `isValidGstin(g)` (shape plus Mod-36 plus state code from DD6). Implement the algorithm inline (89 lines, no new dependency) so the audit team can review every line. Add a unit-test fixture at `tests/gst/gstin.test.js` with 50 real GSTINs (verifiable from public GSTN sources) and 20 deliberately-mangled invalid permutations covering: single-digit typo at position 5, transposed PAN characters at positions 5 and 6, wrong state code (00, 39, 98), wrong entity character (Y instead of Z), padding character mismatch. Sweep `src/api/customers/*.js`, `src/api/suppliers/*.js`, `src/api/tally/companies.js`, `src/api/einvoice/*.js`, `src/api/tally/push.js`, `src/api/tally/amend.js` for any place that accepts a GSTIN-bearing payload and route through `isValidGstin`. Return a 422 with `{ error: { code: "INVALID_GSTIN_CHECKSUM", message: "GSTIN checksum failed" } }` on any failure.

### DD50. cron-mux fan-out and retry semantics; timeout starvation cases

Research outcome. `src/api/_lib/cron-mux.js:67-95` defines `runCronHandler(name, handler, opts)` which wraps a per-handler invocation in a try-catch, captures the synthetic res outcome, and returns `{ name, ok, status, duration_ms, body_preview, error }`. `[verified-on-main]` `runCronGroup(items)` at lines 98-105 wraps the list in `Promise.allSettled` and unwraps. The result is a per-handler outcome array where one handler's failure does not block siblings; the wall-clock budget is the whole-group wait.

There is no per-handler timeout. The relevant gates: `vercel.json:8-10` sets `maxDuration: 60` on `api/dispatch.js`. `[verified-on-main]` Every `/api/cron/*` route runs inside dispatch. If any single handler in the `runCronGroup(SYNCS)` block takes 58 seconds (a slow SAP sync against a misconfigured tenant, an unresponsive Tally bridge, a network partition on an Oracle EBS connection), the remaining 17 sync handlers either run for under 2 seconds total or, more likely, the Vercel function timeout kills the entire tick before they reach their work. The 16 retry-queue drainers in the `RETRIES` block face the same starvation: any single ERP retry that hangs (a Tally bridge with a TCP socket leak, a NetSuite tenant whose OAuth1 signature is stuck rotating) starves every other retry queue. `[verified-on-main: src/api/cron/tick.js:84-128, src/api/_lib/cron-mux.js:67-95]`

The cron heartbeat insert at `recordCronHeartbeat` (lines 126-149) is best-effort and runs after the work, not before; if the function hits Vercel's 60-second wall, the heartbeat for the failing handler is never written. The on-call rotation has no signal beyond the absence of a recent successful tick.

Specific recommendation. Wrap each handler call in `Promise.race([handler(req, res), timeoutPromise(ms)])` where `timeoutPromise` rejects after `ms` and the rejection surfaces in the per-handler result as `error: "timeout"`. Per-handler budgets from a config block in `cron-mux.js`: SAP 25 seconds (the slowest OAuth chain), NetSuite 20 seconds, D365 20 seconds, Acumatica 20 seconds, P21 15 seconds, Eclipse 15 seconds, SXE 15 seconds, Sage X3 15 seconds, Oracle EBS 25 seconds, Oracle Fusion 20 seconds, JDE 25 seconds, Plex 20 seconds, JobBoss 20 seconds, IFS 25 seconds, Ramco 20 seconds, proALPHA 20 seconds, Tally 30 seconds (Tally bridges are on-prem and slowest). Total group budget cap: 50 seconds, leaving 10 seconds of dispatch headroom. Emit `processing_events.event_type = 'cron.handler.timeout'` on every timeout. Write per-handler heartbeats inline (in the result loop) rather than after the group; even on dispatch timeout the partial heartbeat tells the on-call which specific handler died. Add a Vercel deploy-hook smoke test that fires `/api/_healthz` plus a manual `/api/cron/tick` against a staging tenant after every deploy and rolls back on the second consecutive failure.

## Section 6. Risk register and mitigations

Per fix, two risks plus mitigation plus worst-case rollback.

F1 Tally VCHTYPE sweep. Risk A: existing `tally_voucher_records` rows with `voucher_type='SalesOrder'` reflect actual Sales Order vouchers in customer Tally instances; re-emitting as Sales would create a duplicate voucher with different posting. Mitigation: tag legacy rows with `voucher_type_legacy='SalesOrder'` plus add a `migration_104_legacy_so` flag; do not re-emit; communicate to each affected tenant with a CA-signed-off remediation plan. Risk B: TallyPrime sandbox dry-run passes but a specific tenant's chart-of-accounts rejects the new envelope due to missing party ledger or missing CGST ledger. Mitigation: per-tenant pre-flight check that confirms the required ledgers exist before any push hits production. Rollback: feature-flag at `tenant_settings.tally_voucher_kind` defaulting to legacy; switch back to `SalesOrder` for any tenant whose chart-of-accounts rejects.

F2 auth_magic_links RLS. Risk A: backfill from `auth.users` joined on email leaves rows with null tenant_id where the email is not in `auth.users` (pre-tenancy noise, deleted users); the `set not null` migration would fail. Mitigation: delete null rows first under an audit-event record, then `set not null`. Risk B: the strict policy breaks an unannounced caller that depends on the null-tenant-readable behaviour. Mitigation: staging soak with policy-denial logging plus a 7-day grep over Sentry for `rls.deny.magic_link` events before flipping production. Rollback: re-create the permissive policy via a hot-fix migration; rollback time under 5 minutes.

F3 Eval refactor. Risk A: existing `eval_runs` rows lose interpretability if the schema changes mid-flight. Mitigation: tag legacy rows `mode='legacy_caller_asserted'`; dashboard filters on mode. Risk B: golden fixture curation lags engineering and CI fails on coverage rather than accuracy. Mitigation: Phase 1 ships only 20 fixtures with a 2% accuracy gate; Phase 2 expands to 50. Rollback: disable the CI gate at the workflow level if it produces too many false-positive blocks in the first week.

F4 Cron registration. Risk A: Vercel Pro plan upgrade timing slips past Phase 1 (procurement delay). Mitigation: Path B fallback (heartbeat-staleness alert from daily.js) lands in the same PR so reliability improves regardless of plan upgrade. Risk B: cron-job.org and Vercel both fire at the same minute causing double-tick. Mitigation: tick.js is idempotent (per design); a double-tick is safe but wasteful; log the double-fire and alert if it exceeds 10% of ticks. Rollback: remove the Vercel cron entry; cron-job.org carries the load.

F5 Storage tenant scoping. Risk A: backfill misses an object whose `documents` row was deleted but the object survives. Mitigation: quarantine path `<orphan>/<original_path>`; operator triage script confirms each before deletion. Risk B: a browser-direct `supabase.storage.from()` call that the inventory missed breaks at flip. Mitigation: `grep -rln "supabase.storage.from" src/` pre-flight check; route any hits through the API. Rollback: drop the strict policy, re-install the permissive one; rollback time under 10 minutes.

F6 OBARA_STATE removal. Risk A: a tenant has multiple `tally_companies` rows with different GSTINs (e.g. a group with Maharashtra plus Karnataka registrations); the dominant-row backfill picks the wrong one. Mitigation: pre-flight script flags any tenant with mixed-state GSTINs for manual review before backfill. Risk B: the legacy bundler ships with the old constant inlined into a built artefact; the rebuild misses a CDN-cached version. Mitigation: cache-bust the legacy bundler on deploy; force a fresh download. Rollback: re-introduce the constant as a fallback when `tenant_settings.state_code` is null.

F7 Injection bench. Risk A: the bench finds an injection success on `main @ c4f946b` plus Phase 1 changes. Mitigation: that's the point; fix immediately. Risk B: the bench's prompt corpus is biased toward English; misses Hindi or regional-language injection vectors. Mitigation: Phase 2 expands the corpus; Phase 1 ships English-only. Rollback: not applicable (bench is additive).

F8 GSTIN checksum. Risk A: a real-world GSTIN that ICAI issued passes the regex but fails Mod-36 due to a CBIC algorithm version mismatch. Mitigation: 50-real-GSTIN fixture covers this; flag any production failure as `INVALID_GSTIN_CHECKSUM_REAL_WORLD` for triage. Risk B: customer master records with legacy invalid GSTINs cannot create new orders. Mitigation: existing customer master is not retroactively validated; only new orders enforce the check; provide a customer-master cleanup tool. Rollback: feature-flag the strict check; disable on per-tenant basis if needed.

F9 /api/_healthz route. Risk A: the route accidentally exposes internal state (db credentials, integration tokens). Mitigation: the route returns only `{ ok, commit, ts, db_ok, integrations_summary }`; no secret values; integration summary is per-integration boolean only. Risk B: the route becomes a DDoS target. Mitigation: rate-limit at the dispatch layer; cache the response for 5 seconds. Rollback: remove the route from router.js.

F10 Per-handler timeout. Risk A: a legitimately-slow handler (Tally bridge under load) hits the 30-second cap and times out, deferring real work. Mitigation: per-handler budget tuned from production timing data; Tally gets 30s, ERPs get 20 to 25s. Risk B: timeout promises leak as unhandled rejections. Mitigation: `Promise.race` plus explicit `.catch` ensures no unhandled rejections; CI test confirms. Rollback: remove the Promise.race wrap; revert to unbounded handler.

F11 Role enum drift. Risk A: removing `approver` from the API breaks any tenant that has historically used `approver` in their RBAC mapping. Mitigation: backfill any `approver` membership to `sales_manager` (the closest semantic match per `rbac.ts`); audit log every remap. Risk B: adding `operator` to the obara_role enum requires a `commit;` checkpoint that may interact with active transactions. Mitigation: schedule migration outside business hours; staging-first. Rollback: enum value additions are non-destructive; the only rollback path is to revert the API allow-list change.

F12 PREFLIGHT date. Risk A: the date templating leaks into a prompt-cache key and invalidates the cache daily. Mitigation: the cache key derives from prompt-id plus version, not from the rendered text. Risk B: a tenant in a non-IST timezone sees the date one day off. Mitigation: use UTC dates in the prompt; minor cosmetic issue, acceptable. Rollback: revert to the hardcoded string.

F13 tally_companies sweep. Risk A: the AST walker has false positives (e.g. recognises `.eq("id", ...)` chains as missing tenant filter). Mitigation: explicit allow-list in the script for chains that legitimately skip tenant filter (e.g. super-admin endpoints). Risk B: the sweep finds a real bug. Mitigation: that's the goal; fix immediately. Rollback: not applicable.

Worst-case rollback plan summary. Every Phase 1 fix has a feature-flag or migration-level rollback under 10 minutes. The two highest-blast-radius changes are F2 (RLS policy replacement) and F5 (storage policy replacement); both are protected by per-policy drop-and-recreate, audit-event logging, and a documented rollback procedure in `docs/RUNBOOKS/phase-1-rollback.md` (to be created in the W2-A PR).
