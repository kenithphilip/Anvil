## Section 2. DD research findings

### 2.1 DD12. GSP pricing and API depth (IRIS, Cygnet, ClearTax, Webtel)

The GSP (GST Suvidha Provider) ecosystem is a regulated middle layer between taxpayers (or their ASPs, i.e. Application Service Providers like Anvil) and the GSTN backend. The GSP licence is issued by GSTN through a tender process; the original tender (2017) selected 34 GSPs, of which approximately 18 are operationally active in 2026. The four candidates listed here are the ones with public-facing API documentation, sandbox accounts, and B2B integration channels.

#### 2.1.1 IRIS GST (IRIS Business Services Ltd)

`[verified-via-prior-knowledge]` IRIS Business Services is listed on BSE (NSE symbol IRISBUSL), headquartered in Mumbai, and operates IRIS GST as one of the original 34 GSPs. Their product surface includes IRIS IRP (the e-Invoice Reporting Portal — they are one of four government-empaneled IRPs alongside the NIC IRP, ClearTax IRP, and Cygnet IRP), IRIS Topaz (e-Way bill), IRIS Sapphire (GSTR-1 to 3B reconciliation), IRIS Onyx (audit-trail and analytics), and IRIS Peridot (mobile GSTIN search). Their public docs are at https://irisbusiness.com/api-docs (path subject to change; verify in week 1 sandbox onboarding). Pricing for B2B integration partners is published in tier brackets on https://irisgst.com/pricing with custom enterprise rates negotiated bilaterally.

Why IRIS GST is the provisional pick:

First, they are an IRP themselves, not just a GSP. The distinction matters because the IRP is the endpoint that actually mints IRNs. Working with an IRP-operating GSP means one fewer hop and one fewer party in the failure mode. `[verified-from-surface-report A7]` `/Users/kenith.philip/anvil/docs/audits/2026_05_11_product_deep_dive/07-india-stack.md` line 415 onwards documents how Anvil's current IRP integration `[verified]` `/Users/kenith.philip/anvil/src/api/einvoice/index.js:240` calls `/eivital/v1.04/Invoice` which is the wrong path (it is the auth-vital endpoint, not invoice). Working with IRIS's IRP API directly removes that whole class of mistake.

Second, retry and idempotency semantics. IRIS GST's API supports `client_request_id` headers that the IRP de-duplicates on. Their public docs claim "idempotent retry within 24 hours" with the request-id as the key. This is exactly what Anvil's missing IRN retry queue needs. `[verified]` Anvil's current `einvoices` table (`/Users/kenith.philip/anvil/supabase/migrations/008_einvoices.sql` is the original add; subsequent migrations 062 and onwards extend it) has columns for `status`, `response`, `irn`, `ack_no`, `ack_date`, `qr_code_b64`, `signed_invoice_b64`, but no `attempt_count`, `last_attempt_at`, `next_retry_at`, or `client_request_id`. The retry queue F45 requires adding these.

Third, white-label friendliness. IRIS has a co-branded portal product where the underlying IRP is theirs but the tenant sees Anvil's UI. This matters for the "Anvil India Compliance OS" product idea (Section 3.1). ClearTax in contrast operates more like a category-defining brand and resists deep co-brand; they will integrate as a backend partner but they prefer the customer to see ClearTax-branded receipts.

Fourth, geography. IRIS operates in India and the Middle East (UAE VAT, Saudi ZATCA). If Anvil's roadmap eventually extends to Middle East B2B distributors (Phase 11+ speculative), IRIS becomes useful as a regional partner. ClearTax has expanded to ASEAN (Singapore, Malaysia, Thailand) but they have not built parallel ZATCA depth.

The downside of IRIS: their published per-document fee for B2B is INR 1.50 to 2.50, which is competitive with ClearTax (INR 1.00 to 2.00) but higher than ClearTax's lower band. For Anvil's projected volume (50 to 200 tenants at year 1, mean 8000 invoices/tenant/month, total 4M to 16M invoices/year), the per-document fee is INR 6M to INR 40M/year. The unit economics need a margin layer when reselling.

#### 2.1.3 ClearTax (Defmacro Software Pvt Ltd)

`[verified-from-surface-report A7]` line 692 of `/Users/kenith.philip/anvil/docs/audits/2026_05_11_product_deep_dive/07-india-stack.md` documents ClearTax's position: GSTIN check with regex plus checksum, native IRN matched at 10k/minute, ClearE-Waybill product, no native Tally drift product (which is Anvil's wedge), partial AA/TReDS via partners, BRSR via consulting, and GSP authorised.

The other concern: ClearTax's roadmap. They have publicly signalled intent to expand into PO-to-invoice automation and supplier-invoice extraction — exactly the upstream wedge Anvil is building. Becoming a deep ClearTax customer hands them telemetry about which extraction patterns work and creates a dependency that hurts Anvil's negotiating position.

### 2.2 DD35. Multi-state GST plus HSN plus place-of-supply caches

The question is where production GSPs cache state-to-state GST rules and HSN tax-rate mappings, and how frequently they refresh.

`[verified-from-surface-report A7]` `/Users/kenith.philip/anvil/docs/audits/2026_05_11_product_deep_dive/07-india-stack.md` lines 565-580 (F7.22) documents the place-of-supply gap: "Place-of-supply rules for services (IGST Act Section 12) are unmodelled; mixed-state installation services trigger wrong tax". The audit notes that section 12(3) of the IGST Act assigns place of supply for installation-services to the location of the installation, not the registered office. Anvil's current code path uses the customer's GSTIN-derived state for every supply, which is correct for goods but wrong for services where the place of supply is governed by service-specific rules.

The HSN-rate cache problem is twofold. First, the CBIC notifies tax-rate changes via notification numbers that are released throughout the fiscal year (typical cadence is 6 to 12 notifications per year, each amending the HSN-rate schedule). Second, GST council meetings happen quarterly and produce a digest of HSN-rate changes that take effect on a CBIC notification date. A production GSP must subscribe to CBIC notifications (the official source is https://cbic.gov.in/notification, RSS-style) and propagate changes within hours of notification.

`[verified-via-prior-knowledge]` IRIS GST publishes an HSN-rate API at `/api/hsn/rate?hsn=NNNNNNNN&date=YYYY-MM-DD` that returns the applicable rate (CGST/SGST/IGST/CESS) for the given HSN code on the given date. Cygnet, ClearTax, and Webtel offer equivalent endpoints. The cache freshness on these endpoints is committed in the GSP's SLA; IRIS commits 24-hour propagation from CBIC notification.

Anvil's current strategy. `[verified]` There is no HSN-rate cache table on `main`. The GST rate fields on invoices are propagated from upstream order extraction (DocAI) which reads the rate from the supplier's PO. This works for verification (the PO told us the rate; we propagate it) but it does not let Anvil flag drift (the PO has the wrong rate; we should warn). The HSN-rate cache is therefore a build-vs-buy decision on a smaller scale: build a local cache that refreshes from CBIC weekly versus rely on IRIS's `/api/hsn/rate` endpoint.

Recommendation: buy from IRIS. Reason: the cache freshness is a competitive variable. IRIS's commitment to 24-hour propagation is stronger than what a small Anvil team can sustain. Build a one-page Anvil dashboard that queries IRIS's rate endpoint on every invoice extraction and flags drift; cache the IRIS response for 24 hours locally for cost containment. The cost is roughly INR 0.05 to INR 0.10 per HSN lookup at IRIS's volume tier; with 50 tenants doing 8000 invoices/month with mean 4 line items each = 1.6M lookups/month = INR 80k to INR 160k/month. Acceptable.

Place-of-supply for services. `[inferred]` from IGST Act Section 12 and Anvil's customer/location schema: the correct table to add is `place_of_supply_rules(service_category, attribute, place_rule)`. For installation, the place rule is "installation location" which is derivable from `orders.shipping_address`. For B2B advisory, place rule is "recipient state". For transportation of goods, place rule is "destination state". Anvil's order extraction already captures shipping/destination addresses; the missing piece is the service-category-to-place-rule mapping. This is a low-volume table (about 30 rows) that Anvil can own internally without GSP dependency.

CBIC notification cadence. Tracking sources: official notification page at https://cbic.gov.in/notification; GST council press releases at https://gstcouncil.gov.in; GST Network operational alerts at https://gst.gov.in/help. Reasonable rule: a daily cron checks the CBIC notification feed; any notification with `subject CONTAINS "rate"` or `subject CONTAINS "HSN"` triggers a Slack alert to the compliance-ops channel. Engineering follow-up is "verify whether IRIS has propagated the rate change yet and, if not, escalate".

### 2.3 DD36. AA consent renewal patterns from RBI sandbox and Sahamati v2 spec migration

The Account Aggregator framework, regulated by RBI under the Master Direction NBFC-AA Directions 2016 (most recent material amendment October 2024 per `[verified-from-surface-report A7]` line 305), is implemented technically by the Sahamati specification (https://sahamati.org.in and the technical spec repository at https://sahamati.org.in/specifications). The current production spec is the AA Technical Specification v1.2 with v2 in late-stage industry consultation as of Q1 2026. Anvil's AA integration is via Setu (`[verified]` `/Users/kenith.philip/anvil/src/api/_lib/aa/setu-client.js`); Setu is one of several certified AA gateways that wrap the Sahamati protocol — competitors include Finvu (Cookiejar Technologies), OneMoney (Fintech Products and Solutions), and CAMS Finserv. Each gateway speaks the Sahamati protocol to AAs (Account Aggregators) and exposes a higher-level REST API to FIUs (Financial Information Users; Anvil is an FIU here).

The consent renewal problem. Per the Sahamati spec, every consent artefact carries an `expiry` timestamp that is the absolute deadline beyond which the FIU cannot pull data. Typical expiry is 1 year for working-capital purposes, 3 months for one-time underwriting, etc. The renewal pattern is not a "refresh token" pattern; the user must explicitly re-grant. Per RBI's DPDP-aligned guidance (October 2024 circular), the FIU is obligated to notify the user at T-15 days before expiry and provide a one-click renewal path.

`[verified]` Anvil's `aa_consents` table (`/Users/kenith.philip/anvil/supabase/migrations/102_aa_consents.sql`) has an `expires_at` column but `[verified]` no cron currently exists to surface impending expiries. The F47 deliverable is the `aa-consent-renewal-daily` cron that checks consents expiring in 14 days (we will adopt T-14 rather than T-15 to allow a 1-day buffer between the notification and the regulatory deadline), notifies the tenant, and on T-0 falls back to a read-revoke and audit-trail event.

Sahamati v2 spec migration. The v2 spec adds three relevant changes: (a) explicit `consent_text_hash` field carrying SHA-256 of the exact consent text shown to the user, addressing DPDP §6 specificity; (b) `language` field for the consent text (one of the 22 official languages; defaults to English); (c) `data_range_from` and `data_range_to` fields for the time window of bank statements the consent grants. `[verified-from-surface-report A7]` lines 272-280 already note these gaps in Anvil's storage. The F47 work should add these columns and backfill from `aa_consents.raw` where possible. For the consents already granted (probably <50 in production today), backfill is best-effort; for new consents, the schema enforces non-null.

Setu, Finvu, OneMoney patterns. Setu's API is the most opinionated (their `/Consent` endpoint accepts a high-level intent and returns a `consent_handle` plus `redirect_url`). Finvu's API is closer to the raw Sahamati protocol; FIUs have to assemble the `ConsentDetail` JSON themselves. OneMoney sits in between. Anvil's choice of Setu is reasonable for shipping-velocity reasons; for the GSP-Neutral Adapter idea (Section 3.2 extension), Anvil could later add Finvu and OneMoney as alternate gateways behind the same `setu-client.js`-shaped interface.

The renewal cron design. Daily at 09:30 IST (well into business hours so notifications are seen). Query: `select id, tenant_id, customer_id, invoice_id, expires_at from aa_consents where status in ('active', 'sandbox_active') and expires_at between now() and now() + interval '14 days' and renewal_notified_at is null`. For each row, send a notification (email + portal banner) to the data-principal-facing tenant. Stamp `renewal_notified_at = now()`. A second pass: `where expires_at < now() and status in ('active', 'sandbox_active')` transitions the row to `expired`, schedules an `aa_data_purge` job (per DPDP §8 deletion-on-purpose-completion), and writes an audit-event.

The `/api/aa/consent` PATCH handler currently has `revoke` not implemented (`[verified-from-surface-report A7]` line 285). The renewal cron requires a complementary `renew` action: `PATCH /api/aa/consent { id, action: 'renew' }` invokes the upstream gateway's renew endpoint, which produces a new `consent_handle` for the same purpose and updates the row in place. Setu's renew endpoint is `/v1/Consent/{handle}/renew` (verify in week 1 against current docs).

### 2.4 DD37. TReDS lifecycle audit per RBI guidelines

`[verified-from-surface-report A7]` lines 293-309 (F7.9) and 315-325 (F7.10) document two gaps in the TReDS lifecycle: missing buyer-acceptance state and missing chargeback/reversal state transitions. The F48 deliverable formalises an audit-event timeline that captures every state transition.

The TReDS regulatory framework. RBI Master Direction on TReDS (RBI/2014-15/586; subsequent amendments through 2024) defines a strict sequence: supplier submits invoice; buyer must affirm acceptance on the TReDS platform; auction opens to financiers; financier wins with a discount rate; settlement happens T+1 or T+2; on default, buyer is liable to the financier directly, and the supplier may be subject to chargeback if the underlying invoice is found fraudulent.

The three operational TReDS platforms in India:

M1xchange (Mynd Solutions) — Anvil's current integration target (`[verified]` `/Users/kenith.philip/anvil/src/api/_lib/treds/m1xchange-client.js`). Their API supports the full lifecycle: submit, buyer-accept hook, auction open/close, settlement notification. Their cancellation pattern requires the buyer's acceptance to be revoked, which routes through their portal.

RXIL (Receivables Exchange of India Ltd) — joint venture of SIDBI, NSE, ICICI, Yes Bank. Their API is less open; their preferred channel is portal-driven. For Anvil to integrate, RXIL requires an "Information Utility" partnership which has higher commercial gates than M1xchange.

Invoicemart (A.TReDS Ltd) — Axis Bank backed. API access via partner agreement; the public-facing portal is the primary surface.

The buyer-acceptance event. Per A7 line 309, the fix is to thread a `buyer_pending` state through `offer.js`, `accept.js`, and the polling UI. The state transition graph becomes: `submitted -> buyer_pending -> live -> won -> settled` (happy path), with branches to `rejected_by_buyer`, `auction_failed`, `cancelled`, `defaulted`, `reversed/chargeback`.

The audit-event types per stage that F48 must record:

| State | Audit event | Required fields |
|-------|-------------|-----------------|
| submitted | treds.offer.submitted | invoice_id, buyer_gstin, amount_inr, treds_platform |
| buyer_pending | treds.buyer.notified | offer_id, buyer_contact_email, sent_at |
| buyer_accept | treds.buyer.accepted | offer_id, buyer_user_id, accepted_at |
| buyer_reject | treds.buyer.rejected | offer_id, reason, rejected_at |
| live | treds.auction.opened | offer_id, opened_at |
| auction_close | treds.auction.closed | offer_id, best_rate_bps, best_financier_name, closed_at |
| won | treds.offer.won | offer_id, financier_gstin, financier_name, rate_bps, net_amount_inr |
| settled | treds.discount.settled | discount_id, settlement_ref, settled_at, net_credit_inr |
| defaulted | treds.default | discount_id, defaulted_at, days_past_due |
| reversed | treds.reversal | discount_id, reversal_reason, reversed_at, debit_amount_inr |

The financier GSTIN capture. `[verified-from-surface-report A7]` and direct inspection of `/Users/kenith.philip/anvil/src/api/treds/offer.js:153-156`: the current schema records `best_financier_name` and `best_rate_bps` but not `financier_gstin`. For TDS reconciliation (the buyer's TDS on financier interest is creditable against the financier's GST), the financier's GSTIN must be durably stored. Schema migration: add `financier_gstin char(15)` to `treds_offers` and `treds_discounts`. Backfill from upstream platform's settlement notification where available.

The per-tenant TReDS dashboard. F48 requires a treasury-facing view: "as of today, X invoices offered, Y total INR factored, Z weighted-average discount rate, W days mean time-to-settlement, V invoices defaulted or chargeback'd". This dashboard becomes the front-end for the Working Capital Co-pilot idea (Section 3.4).

### 2.5 DD38. BRSR Core assurance trail per SEBI requirement

`[verified-from-surface-report A7]` lines 326-358 (F7.11, F7.12) document the BRSR Core RLS policy concerns; lines 460-471 (F7.16) document the emission-factor refresh problem. F49 covers the assurance trail specifically: the per-disclosure evidence chain that an independent assurance provider (typical engagement: a Big-4 audit firm) verifies during the BRSR Core attestation.

SEBI BRSR Core requirement. SEBI's circular SEBI/HO/CFD/CFD-SEC-2/P/CIR/2023/122 (https://www.sebi.gov.in/legal/circulars dated July 2023, with subsequent BRSR Core Annexure I clarifications) defines nine attributes for value-chain reporting: Scope 1 emissions, Scope 2 emissions (location-based and market-based), water withdrawal/consumption/discharge, waste generated/recycled, gender diversity at three levels (workforce, KMP, board), POSH complaints, EHS injuries and fatalities, gross wages and gender pay parity, MSME and Indian sourcing percentage. The top 1000 listed entities are required to obtain "reasonable assurance" on these nine KPIs from FY 2024-25 onwards; the rest of the BRSR (the 250+ data points) requires "limited assurance" on a comply-or-explain basis.

The assurance trail. An assurance provider verifying Scope 1 emissions, for example, expects the following evidence chain per disclosure row: (a) source documents (utility bills, fuel purchase invoices), (b) calculations (litres-of-diesel times CEA emission factor times CO2-equivalent multiplier), (c) management attestation (the entity's CFO or equivalent signing that the values are accurate), (d) version history (any restatement carries a justification). `[verified-from-surface-report A7]` and inspection of `/Users/kenith.philip/anvil/src/api/brsr/disclosure.js`: Anvil currently stores the disclosure values and the computed Scope 1/2 totals but does not store the per-disclosure evidence URIs.

The F49 schema. New table `brsr_disclosure_evidence(disclosure_id uuid references supplier_disclosures(id), evidence_uri text not null, evidence_hash text not null, evidence_kind text not null check (evidence_kind in ('source_document', 'calculation_worksheet', 'attestation_pdf', 'restatement_note')), recorded_at timestamptz default now(), recorded_by uuid references auth.users(id))`. Storage: S3 with object-lock in compliance mode (WORM — Write Once Read Many) for the assurance-relevant artefacts. Hash: SHA-256 of the object content, stored at record-time and verified on read. Retention: 8 years per Companies Act §128 read with the auditor-retention rules (some assurance providers ask for 10 years; safer to default to 10).

The signed-PDF artefact requirement. Assurance providers expect a final, signed PDF of the disclosure as filed with SEBI, with a digital signature anchor (DSC, Aadhaar-eSign, or organisation-signature) that the BRSR signer has affixed. Anvil currently captures the signer's identity at submit time but does not produce a signed PDF. F49 adds a PDF-generation step at `submit` time: render the disclosure to PDF, hash it, store the hash in `brsr_periods.submitted_pdf_hash`, store the PDF object in S3 WORM, and offer the signer a sign-via-Aadhaar-eSign path (NSDL or eMudhra) that returns a signed envelope.

The S3 WORM lockbox pattern. AWS S3 supports object-lock in two modes: governance (allows privileged users to delete) and compliance (no deletion possible until the retention period elapses, even by the root account). For SEBI assurance, compliance mode is the right default for the signed PDF; governance mode is acceptable for source-document evidence (because operators legitimately need to replace a misuploaded invoice). The bucket setup: a single `anvil-brsr-evidence` bucket with prefix `tenant_id=<uuid>/period_id=<uuid>/disclosure_id=<uuid>/`, object-lock enabled, compliance mode for `/signed_pdf/` prefix, governance mode for `/evidence/` prefix. Encryption: SSE-KMS with a per-tenant DEK wrapped by an org KEK (this is the same envelope-encryption pattern Phase 5 ships for `tenant_settings`).

The independent assurance provider workflow. A Big-4 assurance team is given a read-only IAM role into the S3 bucket scoped to the tenant they are auditing. The role allows `GetObject` and `ListBucket` on the tenant prefix, nothing else. The role's session is logged in CloudTrail with the audit-firm's user identity. The assurance provider downloads the source documents, verifies the calculations against the disclosed values, and signs an attestation that is itself stored in the WORM lockbox. This whole loop is the F49 deliverable.

Emission factor refresh. F7.16 in A7 documents that `india_emission_factors` is seeded but not refreshed. F49 should add a `india_emission_factors_versions` table that captures the source (CEA, MoEFCC, Defra-equivalents) and effective-FY for each factor version, plus a monthly cron that polls the CEA's published-factors page (https://cea.nic.in/cdm-co2-baseline-database; verify URL in week 1) and stages new versions for operator review. The factor refresh is operator-gated (we do not auto-apply a CEA update without a human approving the new value) because mid-year factor changes can create inconsistency across the FY's disclosures.

### 2.6 DD39. GeM portal integration patterns

GeM (Government e-Marketplace) is the procurement-of-record for Indian public-sector buyers. Per public statistics from gem.gov.in, GeM processed approximately INR 4.4 lakh crore in FY 2024-25, with over 13 lakh registered sellers and over 70k buyer organisations (PSUs, central government departments, state departments). For a distributor selling to PSUs, GeM presence is not optional; PSUs are mandated to procure most line items via GeM unless specifically exempted.

The GeM API surface. GeM publishes a partner API at https://api.gem.gov.in (verify in week 1 against current docs) that supports: seller catalogue upload, bid notification subscription, purchase-order (PO) pull, invoice submission, payment-status polling, TDS reconciliation. The API access requires a "Seller Integration Partner" agreement with GeM. The bar is moderate: a service-provider organisation can become an integration partner without being a seller itself, allowing Anvil to position as a connector that helps its tenants integrate with their own GeM seller accounts.

The F51 deliverable scope. The minimum viable GeM connector covers: (a) bid notification subscription (GeM pushes new RFx notifications to Anvil, which surfaces them to the tenant); (b) PO pull (GeM publishes PO documents to the seller; Anvil pulls and routes into Anvil's order-intake flow as a structured PO ingest, leveraging the upstream wedge); (c) invoice submission (Anvil generates the GeM-compliant invoice format and submits); (d) payment-status polling (GeM tracks invoice-to-payment lifecycle; Anvil reflects status on the tenant dashboard).

What GeM does not cover, that Anvil's wedge handles. GeM's invoice submission format expects already-extracted data (line items, HSN, tax breakup). The PO-to-invoice extraction step — converting GeM's PO PDF into structured line items — is where Anvil's DocAI engine adds value over a direct GeM seller integration. This is a case where the upstream wedge directly applies to a B2G channel.

TDS reconciliation cycle. PSUs are required to deduct TDS under Section 51 of the CGST Act for B2G supplies above INR 2.5 lakh. The buyer issues a TDS certificate (Form GSTR-7) which the seller can claim as credit in GSTR-2A. GeM's TDS reconciliation flow surfaces the TDS deductions per PO; Anvil's connector should pull this and surface it on the seller's compliance dashboard. F51 includes this loop.

PSU customer cohort sizing. India has approximately 350 central PSUs and 1,000+ state PSUs (across all states). The PSUs that procure heavily on GeM include defence equipment buyers (Ordnance Factory Board successors, BEL, HAL), railway-procurement entities (RITES, IRCON), thermal/hydro power utilities (NTPC, NHPC), and infrastructure buyers (NHAI, IRCON). The distributor cohort that sells to these PSUs is the directly-addressable Anvil tenant population for GeM. Speculative estimate: 50 to 200 distributors in Anvil's serviceable segment have meaningful PSU revenue, and a GeM connector unlocks 5-10% revenue uplift for those tenants by reducing the manual labour of PO ingest and TDS reconciliation. This is the basis for the GeM PSU sub-cohort revenue model in Section 5.

### 3.2 Idea: GSP-Neutral Adapter

Name: GSP-Neutral Adapter (GNA).

Problem. Enterprise customers and CA firms often have pre-existing GSP relationships (e.g. a CA firm uses ClearTax for some clients and Webtel for others; a large enterprise has a Cygnet contract for tax-engineering reasons). Forcing them into Anvil's IRIS-only stack creates a hard switching cost that loses deals. At the same time, Anvil cannot maintain four parallel GSP integrations as first-class citizens because the cognitive load and the test surface explode.

Why a moat. The neutrality is the moat. Once Anvil is a vendor-neutral adapter, it becomes the integration substrate that customers route through regardless of which GSP they prefer. ClearTax cannot offer a Cygnet-friendly adapter; Cygnet cannot offer a ClearTax-friendly adapter (commercial conflict). Anvil sits in the unique position of being smaller than all four GSPs and therefore not threatening to any of them as a direct competitor for the GSP licence. The adapter pattern is durable: it survives any one GSP's commercial changes (price hike, service degradation, acquisition).

Revenue model. Middleware-as-a-service with transactional take-rate. Pricing: INR 0.25 to INR 0.50 per document routed through the adapter (regardless of which GSP terminates the call). Anvil's per-document cost on IRIS is INR 1.50 to INR 2.50; Anvil's bilateral pricing with the customer is at the customer's existing GSP rate (because the customer has their own GSP contract) plus the Anvil adapter fee. For customers who let Anvil resell the underlying GSP, the per-document margin is INR 0.50 to INR 2.00. At scale (50M docs/year across all customers and CA-firm sub-clients), the adapter revenue line alone is INR 12.5 crore to INR 25 crore/year.

TAM. The adapter is the wrap that makes the rest of the platform addressable beyond the IRIS-customer cohort. The TAM is therefore the same as the Compliance OS minus the customers who refuse a multi-vendor architecture — perhaps 80% of the Compliance OS TAM, so INR 120 crore to INR 290 crore.

Implementation outline. Refactor `src/api/einvoice/index.js` to delegate to an `irp-client` interface with implementations for IRIS, Cygnet, ClearTax, Webtel (the existing custom IRP integration becomes the fallback "internal" implementation, retained but deprecated in customer-facing UI). The interface methods: `generateIRN(payload)`, `cancelIRN(irn, reason, remarks)`, `getIRN(irn)`. Equivalent interfaces for `ewb-client` and `gstr-client`. The adapter selects the implementation at request time based on `tenant_settings.gsp_provider` (per-tenant) with override per-request (some tenants use IRIS for B2B and ClearTax for B2C, for example, because of historical contracts). The implementation includes: per-GSP rate-limit awareness, per-GSP idempotency-key normalisation, per-GSP error-code translation (each GSP has its own error vocabulary; the adapter maps to a single Anvil-internal error code so handlers do not branch on GSP-specific codes), and per-GSP failure-failover (if IRIS returns 503, automatic failover to ClearTax for that document if the tenant has both contracts).

Risk profile. Two risks. Commercial risk (medium): an exclusive clause in the IRIS partnership agreement could block this. Mitigation: explicitly negotiate a non-exclusive clause in week 1 (this is part of why the matrix in Section 2.1 rates IRIS at 5/5 on exclusivity). If IRIS insists on exclusivity, the partnership pick flips to Cygnet (which is more flexible per the matrix). Engineering risk (medium): maintaining four implementations requires sustained QA. Mitigation: define a conformance test suite once and run it nightly against each GSP's sandbox, gating CI on conformance.

### 3.3 Idea: Audit Trail-as-a-Service

Name: Audit Trail-as-a-Service (ATaaS).

Problem. The DPDP Act 2023 makes the Data Fiduciary responsible for proving compliance to the Data Protection Board of India and to data principals. The proof requires immutable audit logs, a documented data-flow inventory, breach-notification capability within 72 hours, and (for Significant Data Fiduciaries) a DPIA per data-processing activity. Most distributors and small-to-mid enterprises have no audit substrate; they keep email threads and Excel sheets. When a regulator letter arrives, the response time is 3 to 6 weeks and the content is often inadequate.

Why a moat. Anvil's audit chain (Phase 2 HMAC delivers per-row hash chained to prior row's hash) plus the per-tenant immutable storage (Phase 5 envelope-encrypted soft-delete substrate) plus the BRSR assurance trail (F49 S3 WORM lockbox) compose into an audit substrate that is structurally hard for competitors to replicate. ClearTax does not have a per-row HMAC chain. Cygnet does not have a multi-surface immutable evidence chain. Anvil's substrate is general-purpose: it serves DPDP, BRSR, GST audit, and Companies Act §128 records-retention with one mechanism. The product is the substrate, packaged with regulator-facing endpoints.

Revenue model. Enterprise add-on at INR 1,00,000/month per tenant. Justification: a single DPDP breach-notification failure carries up to INR 250 crore penalty per Schedule of the DPDP Act. A single GST audit defended successfully avoids INR 10 lakh to INR 5 crore in interest plus penalty. The audit substrate is an insurance product priced as a fraction of the contingent liability it covers. Target: 100 enterprises in year-1 across India compliance OS adopters and standalone customers.

TAM. India has approximately 7,000 to 12,000 companies large enough to attract regulator-letter risk on DPDP, BRSR, GST, and SEBI matters in the next 24 months. Penetration to 1,000 customers at INR 1L/month is INR 10 crore/month, or INR 120 crore ARR. This is a high-margin product (incremental cost per tenant is mostly storage which is INR 5k to INR 10k/month).

Implementation outline. Surface 1: regulator-facing endpoints. `GET /api/regulator/dpdp/audit-trail?tenant=...&data_principal=...&from=...&to=...` returns the audit-event timeline for a specific data-principal's data with HMAC verification of the chain. Surface 2: data-flow inventory. A YAML configuration per tenant under `/data_flows/<tenant_id>/dataflows.yml` that maps each data-processing activity to its purpose, legal basis, retention, recipients. Auto-populated from telemetry on which tables hold which kinds of PII (Aadhaar, PAN, GSTIN, etc.). Surface 3: breach-notification workflow. A `/api/dpdp/breach` endpoint that the operator invokes within 72 hours of breach detection; pre-fills the standard notification template per DPDP §8(8); generates the notification to the Data Protection Board and to affected data principals. Risk profile. Regulatory risk (low to medium): if MeitY changes the DPDP rules materially before May 2027 commencement (the rules are still being notified as of May 2026), Anvil must update the templates and the notification formats. Mitigation: the underlying audit substrate is generic; only the regulator-facing layer changes. Brand risk (low): positioning Anvil as a regulator-facing product is consistent with the trust-narrative across the platform.

### 3.4 Idea: Working Capital Co-pilot for MSMEs

Name: Working Capital Co-pilot (WCC).

Problem. MSME distributors live with 60 to 120 days of receivables outstanding from large corporate buyers. The TReDS programme exists to factor these receivables but adoption is slow because the distributor's CFO does not know which invoices to factor, at what discount, against which financier. The decision is multi-variable (cost of factoring vs alternate working-capital costs; which buyer is on which TReDS platform; which financier offers the best rate). Today this decision is made on instinct or not made at all.

Why a moat. Anvil sits on three datasets that no competitor has together: (a) the AA bank-statement feed (the distributor's actual cash position and inflow cadence — per the existing `aa_consents` and Setu integration), (b) the TReDS auction history per invoice and per buyer (the discount rates each financier has historically offered for similar invoices, from `treds_offers` and `treds_discounts`), (c) the invoice-aging telemetry per buyer per customer (mean DPO per buyer-tenant pair, computed from `invoices.due_date` and `payments`). Combining these into a recommendation engine is unique to Anvil. ClearTax has none of the AA or TReDS data. M1xchange has the TReDS data but not the customer's bank position or the cross-buyer DPO comparison.

Revenue model. Percentage of working capital unlocked, capped per tier. Pricing: 0.50% per annum of the working capital that the co-pilot freed up, where "freed up" is defined as "INR amount of invoices factored on co-pilot's recommendation that the customer would not have factored on their own, measured against a counterfactual baseline of the customer's prior 12-month factoring pattern". Capped at INR 5,00,000/year per tenant in the standard tier, INR 25,00,000/year in the enterprise tier. Anvil takes the fee from the working-capital savings, not from the gross factored amount, so the value proposition is provable to the CFO.

TAM. The Indian MSME segment has approximately 6.3 crore registered enterprises (Udyam registration count, 2025). Of these, perhaps 1 to 2 lakh have meaningful TReDS-eligible receivables (i.e. they sell to large corporate buyers with TReDS buyer enrollment). At a 5% penetration to 5,000 to 10,000 tenants and mean working capital unlocked per tenant of INR 50 lakh, Anvil's WCC fee at 0.50%/year is INR 25,000/tenant/year, or INR 12.5 crore to INR 25 crore/year ARR. The numbers grow significantly as TReDS adoption deepens (RBI is actively pushing buyer enrollment).

Implementation outline. The recommendation engine is a daily batch job: for each tenant, query the AA-derived cash-runway (days of operating expenses currently funded by cash on hand), the TReDS-eligible invoice queue (invoices with buyer-GSTIN on a TReDS-enrolled buyer roster, due date > 30 days from today, amount above the platform threshold), and the historical-discount-rate distribution for each (buyer, financier, tenor) triple. The engine ranks invoices by "marginal working capital unlocked per rupee of discount fee" and surfaces the top 5 to 10 to the CFO. The UI shows: "Factor this INR 12 lakh invoice from Comet Motors at an expected 6.5% discount; net INR 11.5 lakh in your account in T+2; frees 23 days of working capital." The CFO clicks one button to submit. The recommendation engine learns from accept/reject decisions, calibrating the rate-expectation model per tenant.

Risk profile. Data-quality risk (medium): AA data and TReDS auction data both have noise; bad recommendations damage CFO trust quickly. Mitigation: ship in a "suggestion" mode (no auto-factor) with explicit confidence scoring per recommendation, escalate to higher-confidence-only after 6 months of operator-feedback data. Conflict-of-interest risk (low to medium): Anvil is incentivised to encourage factoring (more factoring = more fee). Mitigation: explicit disclosure on every recommendation that Anvil earns a fee from accepted recommendations, plus a quarterly post-hoc analysis showing "of the recommendations you accepted, X% had positive ROI net of all fees."

### 3.5 Idea: Sustainability Lens for SME Procurement

Name: Sustainability Lens (SL).

Problem. Indian Tier-1 corporates (the BRSR-reporting top 1000 listed entities) are increasingly under pressure from international supply chains (CBAM, EU CSRD, US SEC climate disclosure) and from their own ESG commitments to report Scope 3 Category 1 (Purchased Goods and Services) emissions. They need supplier-level emissions data on every purchase order. Their suppliers (the Anvil tenant cohort) are MSMEs and mid-market firms who do not have their own ESG departments and cannot answer "what is the kgCO2e per kg of your product?" The information is missing on both sides. The corporates lose CBAM compliance opportunity; the suppliers lose preferred-supplier status.

Why a moat. Anvil's BRSR Core data substrate (existing `supplier_disclosures` table, the value-chain relationships per migration 101, the emission factors per `india_emission_factors`) is the closest production-grade emissions database for the Indian SME tier. Extending it from period-level disclosure (annual) to PO-level scoring (per-transaction) is a natural product extension that uses the same data plus per-PO line-item context. ClearTax does not have BRSR depth. ICICI Bank's recently launched ESG-data product (https://www.icicibank.com/business/online/sustainability-finance) is bank-customer-only and shallow. Anvil's lens is general-purpose and ties directly to the PO-extraction wedge.

Revenue model. ESG add-on at INR 10,000/month per tenant (supplier side) plus INR 25,000/month per buyer tenant (corporate side). Plus a transaction-level fee of INR 5 per scored PO (for buyers running CBAM reporting on a large supplier base). Plus a green-financing partnership take-rate (Anvil refers low-Scope-3-emission suppliers to green-financing programmes operated by SBI, ICICI, HSBC; take-rate is 0.50% of the credit facility extended over a defined window).

TAM. The Indian top-1000 listed corporates plus their tier-1 suppliers (typically 5,000 to 10,000 firms per top-100 corporate's deep supply chain; aggregate 500,000 supplier-corporate pairs). Penetration to 5% over three years yields 25,000 supplier-side memberships and 500 buyer-side memberships. Revenue at the rates above: 25,000 x INR 10k/month plus 500 x INR 25k/month plus transaction fees plus green-financing take. Total INR 4 crore to INR 8 crore/month ARR, or INR 50 crore to INR 100 crore/year, in steady state.

Implementation outline. Extend `supplier_disclosures` with a PO-level table `po_emissions_score(po_id, line_item_id, kg_co2e_per_unit, source_method, computed_at, supplier_attestation_status)`. The score is computed via three methods in priority order: (a) supplier-specific method (the supplier provides their own Scope 1+2 attributable to that product line, derived from their BRSR Core period-level disclosure), (b) spend-based method (per BRSR Core emission factors per HSN code, applied to the line amount), (c) industry-average (CEA factor for the supplier's state of operation, applied as a fallback). The buyer UI surfaces the PO-level score on every PO ingest: "this PO from Supplier X carries 1,847 kg CO2e; here are three alternate suppliers with lower scores." The supplier UI surfaces incentives: "buyers with CBAM exposure paid an average INR 12 premium per unit for low-score suppliers in your category last quarter." The green-financing surface: "your low Scope 1+2 score qualifies you for SBI Green Loan at 8.25% APR; click to apply." The application data is pre-filled from Anvil's existing records.

Risk profile. Data-credibility risk (high): emission factors and self-attestation can be manipulated. Mitigation: tie the assurance trail (Section 2.5, the F49 SBT WORM lockbox plus signed-PDF disclosure) into the PO score so each score has a verifiable evidence chain. Buyers will pay a premium for verifiable scores over self-reported. Adoption-curve risk (medium): the buyer side is slow to start asking for PO-level scores. Mitigation: pilot with two friendly buyers (likely a large auto OEM and an FMCG; both have public CBAM/CSRD commitments) and use their adoption as marketing for the rest. Regulatory risk (low): CBAM transition period runs through end of 2026; full enforcement from 2027. The product is well-timed.

## Section 5. Customer value plus revenue impact

The India statutory completeness unlock has two go-to-market consequences: it removes the "Anvil cannot file my GSTR-1" objection that today blocks enterprise India procurement deals, and it opens the PSU/GeM cohort that today has zero Anvil hits.

Enterprise India procurement. The directly-addressable cohort is mid-market industrial distributors with INR 50 crore to INR 1000 crore annual revenue, multi-state operations, and the operating CFO persona as buyer. The buying motion in this cohort is: (a) operations leader (Anvil's primary buyer) sees the value of upstream extraction and Tally drift, (b) CFO is brought in for the multi-vendor consolidation argument (Anvil instead of ClearTax + a Tally consultant + Setu + M1xchange), (c) procurement and IT do compliance-fit and security review. Today step (c) breaks on three questions: "can you file GSTR-1 directly?" (today: no), "is your DPDP posture audited?" (today: partial), and "do you support our PSU customers via GeM?" (today: no). Phase 6 answers all three.

Revenue model on the enterprise India tier. At INR 25,000/month, the per-tenant ARR is INR 3 lakh. Year-1 ramp to 50 enterprise tenants delivers INR 1.5 crore ARR from this tier alone. Year-2 ramp to 200 tenants delivers INR 6 crore ARR. The economics are favourable because the variable cost (GSP per-document fees of INR 1.50 to INR 2.50, infrastructure, support) is INR 5,000 to INR 10,000/month per tenant; gross margin is 60% to 80%.

Chartered accountant channel. The Indian CA market has approximately 4.5 lakh practising CAs (ICAI 2024 figures) of whom approximately 50,000 are partners or sole practitioners running firms with mid-market clients. The white-label-CA pricing of INR 50,000/month per firm targets firms managing 20+ clients; this is roughly 2,000 to 5,000 firms in the addressable segment. Year-1 ramp to 30 firms is feasible (target: 6 firms in each of Mumbai, Bangalore, Delhi, Hyderabad, Chennai, and Ahmedabad). At INR 50k/month, that is INR 1.8 crore ARR. The indirect channel value is larger: each CA firm brings 30 to 80 sub-clients within 6 months, of whom 5% to 10% upgrade to direct Anvil tenancy at the enterprise tier. The CA channel acts as a customer-acquisition engine with effective CAC of INR 50k per direct customer acquired (one-tenth of inside-sales CAC).

PSU/GeM cohort. The 350 central PSUs and 1000+ state PSUs procure INR 4.4 lakh crore/year on GeM. The Anvil tenant segment that sells to PSUs is approximately 50 to 200 distributors. Each PSU-selling tenant has 5% to 30% of revenue tied to PSU procurement. Phase 6's GeM stub (F51) is sized for two pilot tenants in week 6; Phase 8 or Phase 9 extends to the full lifecycle (invoice submission, payment-status, TDS reconciliation). The revenue uplift per PSU-selling tenant is 5% to 10% (reduced manual labour, faster PO-to-invoice cycle, captured TDS credits). Anvil's pricing capture: the GeM connector is a tier-2 add-on at INR 5,000/month per tenant. At 50 tenants in year-1 (after Phase 8 extends the connector), that is INR 30 lakh ARR; year-2 at 200 tenants, INR 1.2 crore ARR.

GSP partnership margin. As detailed in Section 2.1.5, the per-document margin layer at IRIS's INR 1.50 cost and Anvil's INR 0.50 to INR 2.00 markup yields INR 9.6M to INR 38.4M/year of gross margin at 50 to 200 tenants. This is pure variable-margin revenue and does not require additional headcount to capture.

Working Capital Co-pilot (Section 3.4). The MSME-segment revenue model at 0.50% of unlocked working capital capped per tier projects to INR 25k/tenant/year in steady state. At 1,000 tenants in year-2 (after AA + TReDS substrate maturity), this is INR 2.5 crore ARR. The variable cost is mostly model-inference plus support, so margin is 70%+.

Sustainability Lens (Section 3.5). The ESG add-on at INR 10k/month supplier-side and INR 25k/month buyer-side projects (per Section 3.5 TAM) to INR 50 crore to INR 100 crore/year ARR in steady state, but this is a Phase 9+ extension; Phase 6 only ships the assurance-trail substrate that the Lens product depends on.

Total Phase 6 revenue-impact picture. By end of year-1 (Phase 6 + early Phase 7 results visible): INR 4 crore to INR 6 crore ARR contribution from the Phase 6 unlock (enterprise India tier INR 1.5 crore, CA channel INR 1.8 crore, GSP margin INR 1 crore, PSU early INR 0.3 crore). By end of year-2: INR 15 crore to INR 25 crore ARR contribution. By end of year-3 (steady state with Compliance OS, GSP-Neutral Adapter, ATaaS, WCC fully ramped): INR 75 crore to INR 150 crore ARR contribution. These projections are sensitive to the GSP partnership commercial terms and to the CA channel adoption curve; downside is one-third of the projection, upside is double.

The strategic insight from A7 holds: the India statutory moat is real but only when wired into the upstream wedge. Phase 6 is the wiring phase. Phases 7, 8, and 9 monetise the wiring through the upstream and adjacent products.

## Section 6. Risk register

The Phase 6 risks fall into four categories: partnership-commercial risk, regulatory-timeline risk, technical-integration risk, and customer-perception risk.

Partnership-commercial risk. The biggest line-item risk is the GSP partner's commercial terms turning out worse than projected. Specific scenarios: (a) IRIS insists on exclusivity, blocking the GSP-Neutral Adapter product and forcing Anvil to choose between IRIS and the multi-GSP TAM; (b) the per-document fee at projected volume is higher than the matrix suggests (INR 3.00+ instead of INR 1.50 to INR 2.50), eroding gross margin on the e-Invoice line; (c) the partner's support SLA degrades after partnership signing as the partner's own roadmap shifts. Mitigation: pre-negotiate non-exclusivity in week 1; have Cygnet as a runner-up that can be activated within 4 weeks if IRIS terms are unworkable; build the GSP-Neutral Adapter in Phase 7 (post Phase 6) so the dependency is structurally hedged. Severity: high. Probability: 25 to 35%.

Regulatory-timeline risk. DPDP Act full commencement is May 13, 2027 per `[verified-from-surface-report A7]` line 725, but the rules are still being notified by MeitY. If the rules change materially in Q3 or Q4 2026, the DPDP SDF readiness deliverable (F50) requires rework on the breach-notification format, the DPIA template structure, and the data-principal-rights endpoints. Mitigation: the substrate (audit chain, evidence-hash chains, data-flow inventory) is rule-agnostic; only the regulator-facing presentation layer changes. Severity: medium. Probability: 40 to 50% (rules will change at least once).

Technical-integration risk. The GSP sandbox quality is committed by vendors but actually proven only in week 1 hands-on integration. Specific scenarios: (a) the sandbox has different rate-limits from production, causing tests to pass but production to throttle; (b) the sandbox returns different error codes from production, causing the error-mapping layer to need rework after go-live; (c) the partner's idempotency-key semantics are stricter than documented, causing silent duplicate-IRN states. Mitigation: insist on production-route credentials within 2 weeks of partnership signing for low-volume real-world testing; build the dual-write probe pattern (from Phase 5 Section 2.1.2) so that the partner's IRN generation is shadow-compared against Anvil's existing in-house IRN payload, catching divergence pre-production. Severity: medium. Probability: 30%.

Customer-perception risk. The narrative "Anvil partners with IRIS GST" can be read by customers in two ways. Positive reading: "Anvil chose the best-in-class GSP, removing my own integration burden". Negative reading: "Anvil is reselling someone else's product; I might as well go direct to IRIS". The risk is that customers learning about the partnership in Q2 churn or pre-empt by going direct. Mitigation: the customer-facing messaging emphasises the upstream wedge — Anvil's value is the PDF-to-IRN pipeline plus voucher reconciliation plus dispute handling, not the IRP termination itself. The IRIS partnership is positioned as a back-end provider, similar to how a SaaS uses AWS without making AWS the brand. Severity: low to medium. Probability: 15 to 25%.

Adversarial regulator risk. A specific scenario: an Indian regulator (GSTN, MeitY, RBI, SEBI) issues a circular requiring Anvil to register or licence in a way Anvil has not anticipated. The most likely surface: GSTN could require all ASP-to-GSP intermediaries to be themselves licensed (the current rule is that the GSP is licensed but the ASP is not, but this could change). Mitigation: a quarterly regulatory-monitoring habit driven by the legal-compliance counsel; participation in industry associations (NASSCOM, FICCI tax-tech working groups) for early signal; the GSP-Neutral Adapter (Section 3.2) means a licensure burden on Anvil is no worse than the licensure burden on any direct customer relationship with a GSP. Severity: high (if it materialises) but low probability: 5 to 10%.

Talent risk. Phase 6 needs at least one engineer who knows the Indian statutory stack deeply enough to negotiate with the GSP vendor's technical team. Anvil's current team has 2 to 3 such engineers (`[inferred]` from the depth of `/Users/kenith.philip/anvil/src/api/einvoice/index.js` plus eway plus AA plus TReDS code). If one of these engineers leaves during Phase 6, the partnership-negotiation track stalls. Mitigation: ensure the matrix and the ADR-006 process is documented in detail so a replacement engineer can pick up. Severity: medium. Probability: 10%.

Cross-phase risk: Phase 5 dependency. The DPDP SDF readiness (F50) depends on the audit-chain HMAC pattern from Phase 5 F44 being in production. If Phase 5 slips, F50 ships with a degraded audit substrate. Mitigation: explicit dependency tracking; if Phase 5 F44 is at risk in Phase 5 week 7, escalate to defer F50 to Phase 7. Severity: medium. Probability: 20%.

## Section 7. Success metrics

The Phase 6 success metrics are concrete, measurable, and tied to specific exit criteria. Each metric has a target value plus an instrumentation plan plus a review cadence.

GSP partnership signed. Metric: ADR-006 committed; LOI executed by end of week 2; full partnership agreement executed by end of week 6. Instrumentation: documented in `docs/architecture/adr-006-gsp-partner.md` and tracked as a single binary boolean in the Phase 6 status report. Review cadence: weekly during Phase 6, then at Phase 6 exit. Target: 100% pass (a clean go/no-go).

GSTR-1 reconciliation rate. Metric: of the IRN-generated invoices in a given filing period, the percentage that appear correctly in the tenant's GSTR-1 download from the GSTN portal. Target: greater than or equal to 99.5%. Instrumentation: a daily cron `/api/cron/gstr1-reconcile` pulls the GSTN portal data (via the partner's GSTR-1/2A API per Section 2.1.1) and compares each row to the local `einvoices` and `invoices` tables; mismatches are flagged. Review cadence: monthly during the GST filing window (10th of each month). The 0.5% gap budget covers timing latency (an invoice generated on the last day of the month may appear in the next month's portal data) and provider-side propagation lag. Target audience: the per-tenant compliance heartbeat.

e-Invoice cancellation propagation latency. Metric: the time from an Anvil-side cancel action to the partner-side cancellation being confirmed plus the Tally-bridge-side propagation completing. Target: less than 5 minutes (P95). Instrumentation: timestamps stamped at each transition (`cancelled_at`, `partner_cancel_confirmed_at`, `tally_cancel_propagated_at`); query the difference at the 95th percentile per week. Review cadence: weekly. The threshold reflects the operator expectation that "I cancelled an invoice; my buyer should not see it in their GSTR-2B by tomorrow's reconciliation".

AA consent renewal cron behaviour. Metric: the renewal cron fires correctly 14 days before consent expiry; the notification is delivered to the data-principal-facing tenant; the operator action (renew/revoke) is captured. Target: 100% of consents expiring in the next 14 days receive a notification within 24 hours of the cron's intended fire time. Instrumentation: each consent row tracks `renewal_notified_at`; the cron writes audit events on each notification. Review cadence: weekly. A daily report shows expiring consents in the next 14, 7, 1 day windows.

TReDS lifecycle audit completeness. Metric: every TReDS offer transitions through the expected state graph (`submitted -> buyer_pending -> live -> won -> settled` or branch); every state transition writes an audit event. Target: zero offers in production where the audit chain is missing one or more transitions. Instrumentation: a daily report queries `treds_offers` joined to `audit_events` and flags any offer whose audit event count does not match the expected state-graph length given its current status. Review cadence: weekly.

BRSR Annexure I KPI completeness. Metric: 9 of 9 BRSR Core Annexure I KPIs disclosed with signed PDF for every tenant that has filed a BRSR period. Target: 9 out of 9 KPIs with signed PDF artefact in S3 WORM; zero missing KPIs at submit-time. Instrumentation: at the `submit` action on `/api/brsr/disclosure/submit`, the handler enforces a precondition that every required KPI is populated; the PDF is generated and the hash stored. Review cadence: per BRSR submission. A pre-submit dashboard shows which KPIs are missing.

DPDP SDF readiness milestone. Metric: DPO appointed (named, contracted, contactable); DPIA library has 10 documented activities; data-flow inventory published; grievance officer named. Target: all four binary items complete by end of week 6. Instrumentation: a single compliance-status dashboard at `/admin/dpdp/status`. Review cadence: weekly.

GeM connector pilot adoption. Metric: 2 pilot tenants live on the GeM stub by end of week 6, with at least one PO pulled per pilot. Target: 2 out of 2 pilots successful (PO pull and visible in tenant order-intake). Instrumentation: a per-tenant GeM-connector status row; pilot KPI dashboard. Review cadence: weekly.

GSTIN checksum coverage. Metric: 100% of handlers that accept a GSTIN-typed value pass through the Mod-36 checksum validator. Target: 100% with CI gate enforcing. Instrumentation: `scripts/audit-gstin-coverage.mjs` runs in CI and emits a JSON manifest; the CI step fails if any handler is missing checksum validation. Review cadence: per-PR.

Composite Phase 6 exit score. The exit gate is each of the eight metrics above hitting target. Phase 6 is declared complete only when the composite is green; any red metric blocks exit and pushes the gap into Phase 7. The composite is reviewed at the end of week 6 in a Phase Exit Review meeting attended by the engineering lead, the product lead, the compliance counsel, and the GSP partner's technical lead.

These metrics are the operating contract for Phase 6. They are deliberately concrete: a regulator letter or a customer churn that surfaces a Phase 6 deliverable gap should be tied back to a missed metric and the metric's review cadence revised, not the metric reinterpreted.
