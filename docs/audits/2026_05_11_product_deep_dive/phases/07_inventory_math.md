## Section 2. Deep-dive research findings

### Section 2.1. DD15. Block CP, MAPIE, autocorrelated residuals

[tag: dd15] [tag: block-cp] [tag: autocorrelation]

Anvil's `nexCP` implementation at `/Users/kenith.philip/anvil/src/api/_lib/inventory/conformal.js:112-130` is a faithful port of the Barber, Candes, Ramdas, Tibshirani 2023 NEXCP paper (AOS, https://www.stat.cmu.edu/~ryantibs/papers/nexcp.pdf). It assigns exponentially-decaying weights `w_i = rho^(n - 1 - i)` to time-ordered residuals and takes the weighted absolute quantile. The math is correct for sequences whose distribution drifts smoothly but whose residuals remain conditionally exchangeable given the past. The math is silently wrong when residuals are autocorrelated, which is the dominant regime for seasonal weekly-demand series on industrial spares, ATD assemblies, and consumables in `item_master`. The proof is in Chernozhukov, Wuthrich, Zhu 2018 ("Exact and Robust Conformal Inference Methods for Predictive Machine Learning with Dependent Data", https://arxiv.org/abs/1802.06300): when residuals exhibit positive autocorrelation `rho_acf > 0`, the empirical absolute quantile underestimates the true quantile of the marginal distribution. The miscoverage on the upper tail can be 8 to 15 percentage points worse than the nominal `1 - alpha` budget. In Anvil's deployed code path, an ATD assembly with quarterly seasonality (4-week sales cycles, 12-week procurement cycles) will exhibit residual autocorrelation in the 0.3 to 0.6 range. The 95 percent nominal interval will deliver 80 to 87 percent realized coverage. Operators see "this part stocked out, but the conformal band said 95 percent." Trust collapses.

The fix is Block CP. Chernozhukov 2018 proposes blocking the residual sequence into chunks of length `b` (where `b` is roughly the autocorrelation horizon), constructing the conformal quantile on the block-permuted distribution, and inverting back. For a series with autocorrelation horizon 4 weeks and 100 weeks of history, `b = 8` and 12 blocks. The python reference implementation is MAPIE (https://mapie.readthedocs.io/), specifically `mapie.regression.MapieTimeSeriesRegressor` with the `EnbPI` and `block` strategies. MAPIE ships in scikit-learn idiom; Anvil ships pure JS. Porting MAPIE to JS is tractable. The core algorithm is roughly 80 lines: sort residuals within blocks, permute block indices, compute the absolute quantile of the block-aggregated residuals, return. The detection step is `acf(residuals, lag=1)` plus the Durbin-Watson statistic; both are pure-math primitives we can inline in `/Users/kenith.philip/anvil/src/api/_lib/inventory/conformal.js`. The routing rule: if `dw < 1.5` or `acf_lag1 > 0.2`, route to Block CP; else NEXCP.

The Chernozhukov 2018 paper also clarifies the fixed-design regime. NEXCP's exchangeability assumption is "approximate" under smoothly drifting distributions. Fixed-design conformal inference (Chernozhukov et al, section 3) drops exchangeability entirely and provides coverage guarantees on absolute-error pivots under stationarity. The MAPIE library exposes this as `MapieTimeSeriesRegressor(method="enbpi")`. The trade-off: enbpi requires `B` bootstrap replicates of the forecaster (in our case, the Croston / SBA / TSB chain in `/Users/kenith.philip/anvil/src/api/_lib/inventory/forecast.js`), which is `O(B*N)` more compute. For weekly cron jobs on tenants with under 5000 SKUs, this is bearable: 50 replicates times 5000 SKUs at 100 weeks each is 25 million cheap arithmetic ops, well under one minute on a Vercel function. The implementation cost is the JS port.

Anvil's current Split CP fallback for SKUs with 12 to 25 residuals also silently undercovers under autocorrelation. The remedy is the same Block CP routing applied earlier in the residual-count hierarchy. The pooled cold-start CP at `/Users/kenith.philip/anvil/src/api/_lib/inventory/conformal.js:138-150` is even more fragile: it pools residuals across SKUs in the same `item_type`, but as DD16 below explains, the cohort key is wrong, so the pooled distribution mixes autocorrelation structures and the empirical quantile is unstable.

The recommendation for Phase 7: ship `blockCP(residuals, alpha, blockSize)` in `conformal.js`, add a Durbin-Watson detector and an autocorrelation estimator, route in `selectAndComputeCP` when `n >= 26` and `acf_lag1 > 0.2`, and surface the routing decision in the `forecast_runs.notes` field so operators can audit which SKUs went through which CP path. The fix is two engineering days plus math review. The expected coverage improvement on seasonal items is 5 to 8 percentage points, closing the gap between claimed and realized coverage on the cohort that drives most of the working-capital exposure.

A subtler concern is the interaction between Block CP and NEXCP weighting. NEXCP's exponential decay (`rho = 0.99` in `conformal.js:39`) is designed to handle distribution drift. Block CP's permutation argument requires stationarity within blocks. The two assumptions are not contradictory but they target different failure modes: NEXCP handles smooth drift, Block CP handles temporal dependence. A correct implementation may need both: weight blocks exponentially by recency, then conformal-quantile across the weighted blocks. The reference is Xu and Xie 2021 ("Conformal prediction interval for dynamic time-series", https://arxiv.org/abs/2010.09107), which introduces "EnbPI with weights." Anvil's Phase 7 implementation should pick the simpler of the two (weighted Block CP) and defer EnbPI to Phase 8 once we have telemetry on which failure mode dominates.

A second subtler concern is sample size. The Chernozhukov 2018 finite-sample coverage bound for Block CP is `1 - alpha - O(b / sqrt(n))` where `b` is block size and `n` is residual count. For `b = 8` and `n = 100`, the slack is roughly 0.08 / sqrt(100) = 0.008, comfortably under one percentage point. For `b = 8` and `n = 30`, the slack is 0.015, two and a half percentage points. SKUs with fewer than 50 residuals should not be routed to Block CP; they should fall through to the pooled cohort path, which itself benefits from the cohort-key fix in DD16.

### Section 2.2. DD16. Cohort design for inventory pooling

[tag: dd16] [tag: cohort-pooling] [tag: abc-xyz]

The pooled cold-start CP in `/Users/kenith.philip/anvil/src/api/_lib/inventory/conformal.js:138-150` is keyed on `item_type`: ATD, TIMER, GUN, GUN_COMPONENT, SPARE, CONSUMABLE, OTHER. The roadmap document at `14-final-roadmap.md:830` correctly identifies the bug: `item_type` mixes motion classes. A SPARE can be smooth-monthly demand or it can be lumpy-quarterly demand. Pooling them produces a multimodal residual distribution. The empirical absolute quantile of a multimodal distribution is unstable: small changes in pool composition shift the quantile dramatically. Cohort drift kills pooled CP. The fix is to key on `(family, value_class, motion_class)` instead of `item_type`.

The literature backing is the ABC-XYZ classification (Roland Dittrich, 1956; later popularized by Klaus Backhaus and others in the SCM literature) and the value-motion matrix (Croston 1972, Syntetos and Boylan 2005). ABC is a value classification: A items are the top 70 percent of inventory value (typically the top 10 percent of SKUs), B items the next 20 percent of value (next 20 percent of SKUs), C items the remainder. XYZ is a motion classification: X items have CV2 below 0.49 (smooth), Y items have CV2 between 0.49 and 1.0 (erratic), Z items have CV2 above 1.0 (intermittent or lumpy). The combined nine-cell matrix (AX, AY, AZ, BX, BY, BZ, CX, CY, CZ) is the canonical cohorting key in academic and practitioner SCM work for the last 30 years.

Anvil already has the motion class. The `classify.js` file at `/Users/kenith.philip/anvil/src/api/_lib/inventory/classify.js` produces `demand_class in {smooth, intermittent, lumpy, erratic, new}` per Syntetos-Boylan-Croston (ADI threshold 1.32, CV2 threshold 0.49). This is the motion axis. The value axis is missing. The standard recipe: rank items by `purchase_price * annual_demand`, take the cumulative-revenue cutoff at 70 / 90 / 100 percent. The cohort key becomes `(item_type, value_class in {A, B, C}, motion_class in {X, Y, Z})`. For a typical industrial-distributor tenant with 5000 SKUs, this produces roughly 60 cohorts (not all cells populated). Each cohort contains 30 to 200 items, enough for a stable pooled residual estimate.

The family axis is what `item_type` already provides plus a hierarchical roll-up. A GUN and a GUN_COMPONENT share a family. An ATD and an ATD-spare share a family. The roll-up rule is: pool within the same family before pooling across families. Hadley-Whitin section 4.3 discusses this as the "demand-correlation cohort" insight: items in the same family share demand drivers, so their residuals share variance structure.

The proposed schema change for Phase 7 F60 is a migration adding `item_master.value_class` and `item_master.motion_class` columns (both as text, populated by the planning cron) and an index on `(tenant_id, family, value_class, motion_class)`. The `pooledColdStartCP` function changes its lookup from `residualsByClass[item_type]` to `residualsByClass[family + ":" + value_class + ":" + motion_class]`. The fallback hierarchy: own residuals -> 9-cell cohort -> motion-class-only cohort -> global tenant pool. Cohort drift is detected by a Chi-squared two-sample test on the residual distribution between the current week's pool and the prior week's pool. When drift is detected, the engine flags `forecast_runs.notes` and refuses to pool, falling back to wider cohorts. The math is two engineering days, the migration is one day, the chi-squared detector is half a day.

Why does cohort drift matter at the operator level? An industrial-distributor tenant onboards a new product line in Q2. The product line adds 800 new SKUs to the SPARE `item_type`. The new SKUs have a different demand distribution from the existing SPARE pool. Pre-fix, the pooled cold-start CP for those new SKUs is computed against a stale, irrelevant pool. The realized coverage on the new SKUs is 70 percent against a claimed 95 percent. Operators see stockouts on the new line, lose trust in the recommendations, override safety stock manually, working-capital release evaporates. Post-fix, the new SKUs are pooled within their own family-value-motion cell, the residual estimate is stable, the realized coverage matches the claim.

### Section 2.3. DD17. STL, Prophet, Croston-X for monthly seasonal demand

[tag: dd17] [tag: forecasting] [tag: seasonality]

`/Users/kenith.philip/anvil/src/api/_lib/inventory/forecast.js` implements Croston, SBA, TSB, SMA, and SES. The selector at line 143-152 routes intermittent -> SBA, lumpy -> TSB, erratic -> SES, smooth -> SMA-4, new -> SMA-4. The forecast model menu is missing two regimes: explicit seasonality and trend-plus-seasonality. For weekly-bucketed demand on monthly-seasonal items (HVAC parts, agricultural sprayers, festival-driven consumables), the baseline mean from a flat smoother like SBA or SMA understates demand in peak weeks and overstates in trough weeks. The downstream CP band is centred on the wrong mean. Coverage suffers symmetrically: high in troughs (band too wide), low in peaks (band too narrow). The Phase 2.5 comment at `forecast.js:8-9` already acknowledges this gap: "The full ML model menu (NHITS / TFT / LightGBM) defers to a Python micro-service in a Phase 2.5 follow-up."

The classical literature on seasonal weekly demand splits into three camps. STL decomposition (Cleveland 1990, "STL: A Seasonal-Trend Decomposition Procedure Based on Loess", J Off Stat) decomposes a series into trend, seasonal, and remainder components using local-polynomial smoothers; the residual component is then modeled with a stationary forecaster (often ARIMA or SES); the seasonal and trend components are added back at forecast time. STL is the workhorse for monthly seasonality in statsmodels (`statsmodels.tsa.seasonal.STL`). Prophet (Taylor and Letham 2017, "Forecasting at Scale", The American Statistician) is the Facebook-now-Meta library that fits a generalized additive model with piecewise-linear trend, multiple Fourier-basis seasonality terms, holiday effects, and Hamiltonian MCMC posterior inference. Prophet dominates when seasonality is multi-scale (weekly plus annual) and when irregular events (festivals, monsoon, ICC matches) drive demand. STL beats Prophet on series with one dominant seasonal cycle and a stable trend.

The third camp is intermittent-demand specific: Croston-X (Boylan and Babai 2016, "On the Performance of Overlapping and Non-Overlapping Temporal Demand Aggregation Approaches", IJPE) overlays Croston-style smoothing on STL-decomposed remainders. The SBA dominance over Croston for ADI > 1.0 is well-established: Syntetos and Boylan 2005 ("The Accuracy of Intermittent Demand Estimates", IJPE Vol 88) showed SBA delivers 5 to 15 percent lower MAPE than vanilla Croston when ADI > 1.32. Anvil already routes intermittent items to SBA at `forecast.js:145`, which is correct. The gap is TSB. The Teunter-Syntetos-Babai forecaster (Teunter, Syntetos, Babai 2011, "Intermittent Demand: Linking Forecasting to Inventory Obsolescence", EJOR) smooths the probability of a non-zero event and the size given a non-zero event independently. TSB dominates SBA when obsolescence is a concern (items with declining demand probability), which is most spares with a 10-year tail. Anvil routes lumpy items to TSB at `forecast.js:146`, which is correct, but the routing is by demand class, not by obsolescence detection. A lumpy item without obsolescence risk does not need TSB; a smooth item with obsolescence risk does. The routing should detect probability decay over the last 12 months and route to TSB when the slope is negative-and-significant.

The recommendation for Phase 7 is conservative: ship STL decomposition as an opt-in for tenants with explicit seasonality flags (`item_master.seasonal_flag = true`), and surface the routing decision in `forecast_runs.models_evaluated`. STL in pure JS is roughly 200 lines (loess smoother + seasonal mean by week-of-year). Prophet is a heavier lift (Python micro-service deferred to Phase 8); the value-add over STL for weekly demand on industrial items is marginal. Croston-X is a 50-line wrapper combining STL-decomposed remainder with SBA on the remainder. The expected MAPE improvement on seasonal SKUs is 10 to 25 percent, and the downstream coverage improvement on the CP band is 2 to 4 percentage points (the band is narrower because the residual variance is lower).

The integration with conformal inference matters. Block CP from DD15 assumes stationary residuals after the forecaster has explained the systematic structure. STL plus SBA on the remainder produces a more nearly-stationary residual sequence than vanilla SBA on the raw series. So STL is a precondition for Block CP to deliver its coverage guarantee on seasonal items. The dependency ordering for Phase 7: ship STL (week 3), then ship Block CP (week 5), then validate coverage on the backtest harness (week 6). The full chain is then forecaster decomposition -> stationary residuals -> Block CP -> claimed coverage = realized coverage. Without the STL step, Block CP on seasonal items will still undercover.

A practitioner-level note on the SBA-versus-Croston comparison. The empirical literature (Petropoulos et al 2014, "Forecasting with temporal hierarchies", EJOR, plus the M5 competition results from 2020) consistently finds SBA dominating Croston for ADI in the 1.32 to 4.0 range. Above ADI 4.0 (truly lumpy demand: spares ordered once a year or less) both forecasters degrade and TSB starts winning because TSB models the probability of any demand event at all. Below ADI 1.32 (frequent demand: weekly or near-weekly) neither Croston nor SBA is needed and simple exponential smoothing wins. Anvil's classification thresholds at `classify.js` (ADI 1.32, CV2 0.49) place items into the SBA-dominant region by design. The routing logic at `forecast.js:143-152` is correct. The bug is not in the routing; it is in the absence of a seasonality-aware path for the items that are SBA-routed but also seasonal.

A second practitioner note on the Prophet-versus-STL comparison. Prophet's biggest selling point is calendar-event handling (holidays, festivals, sporting events). For Indian industrial demand, the relevant calendar events are: Diwali (October-November, 15 days of dampened demand for non-festival consumer items), Holi (March, similar), Republic Day and Independence Day (single-day bank holidays with no real demand effect on B2B inventory), the monsoon cycle (June through September, agricultural-equipment demand spikes), and the financial year-end (March 31, procurement spikes as budgets close). STL captures the annual cycle implicitly. Prophet captures it explicitly and lets operators add custom regressors for specific calendar dates. For Anvil's customer base, Prophet's calendar-event handling is a real value-add for agricultural-equipment and festival-consumer-distribution customers; STL is sufficient for most industrial-distribution customers. The Phase 7 recommendation is STL as default; Prophet (or its statsmodels-equivalent SARIMAX with calendar regressors) as an opt-in tenant setting `tenant_settings.inventory_seasonality_engine in {auto, stl, prophet}`. The Python micro-service for Prophet/SARIMAX is the Phase 2.5 follow-up referenced in `forecast.js:8-9`; Phase 7 ships only the STL path.

### Section 2.4. DD18. Newsvendor cu/co cost factoring per item

[tag: dd18] [tag: newsvendor] [tag: cost-factoring]

Anvil's safety-stock formula at `/Users/kenith.philip/anvil/src/api/_lib/inventory/safety-stock.js:61-65` uses `z(alpha) * ltdSigma` where `alpha` is a service level. The service level is hardcoded per item class in `/Users/kenith.philip/anvil/src/api/cron/inventory-planning-weekly.js:40-45`: ATD and TIMER get 0.99, GUN and GUN_COMPONENT get 0.95, SPARE and CONSUMABLE get 0.85, OTHER gets 0.95. This is a cycle-service-level (CSL) parameterization. CSL ignores per-item underage and overage costs. Two SKUs with identical demand statistics but radically different cost structures get the same safety stock. This is wrong by 30 years of inventory theory.

The textbook fix is the newsvendor critical-fractile formula, Hadley and Whitin 1963, "Analysis of Inventory Systems" Chapter 4. The critical fractile is `cu / (cu + co)` where `cu` is the underage cost (stockout cost per unit, including lost margin, expediting cost, customer-trust impairment) and `co` is the overage cost (holding cost per unit per period, including capital cost, storage cost, obsolescence risk). The optimal order quantity for a single-period newsvendor problem is the `cu / (cu + co)` quantile of the demand distribution. For a multi-period continuous-review (s, S) policy, the same critical fractile derivation governs the reorder-point quantile (Hadley and Whitin Chapter 5, also Silver, Pyke, Peterson 2017 "Inventory and Production Management" Chapter 8).

Why does this matter for Anvil? Consider two ATD assemblies. ATD-A has unit cost INR 50,000, three-week procurement lead time, customer is a Tier-1 OEM with a stockout penalty of INR 200,000 per shortage (contractual). ATD-B has unit cost INR 50,000, three-week procurement lead time, customer is a Tier-3 industrial customer with a stockout penalty of INR 10,000 (lost margin only). Under Anvil's current hardcoded 0.99 service level for ATDs, both items receive z(0.99) = 2.33 sigma of safety stock. Under the newsvendor optimum: ATD-A has `cu / (cu + co) = 200000 / (200000 + 50000 * 0.22) = 0.948` if holding cost is 22 percent; z(0.948) = 1.625; so 30 percent less safety stock is optimal. ATD-B has `cu / (cu + co) = 10000 / (10000 + 11000) = 0.476`; z(0.476) is approximately -0.06, meaning the newsvendor solution is to hold no safety stock and let the stockout happen. The hardcoded 0.99 over-stocks ATD-B by 100 to 200 percent. Working capital tied up in ATD-B is pure waste.

The fix is `item_master.underage_cost` and `item_master.overage_cost` columns plus a UI affordance in `/Users/kenith.philip/anvil/src/v3-app/screens/inventory-item.tsx` for per-item override. The safety-stock formula becomes `z(cu / (cu + co)) * ltdSigma` when `cu` and `co` are present, falling back to `z(service_level) * ltdSigma` when they are not. The migration is one column-add. The UI is two new fields. The formula change is three lines in `safety-stock.js`. The hard work is operator training: explaining why two identical-looking ATDs now hold different safety stocks. The Phase 7 deliverable includes a one-page operator-facing explainer in `inventory-item.tsx` rationale tooltip showing the cu/co arithmetic.

The conformal-prediction interaction is subtle. Anvil's CP band is symmetric: `qLo = -q, qHi = +q`. The newsvendor optimum is asymmetric: the upper-tail probability matters more for high-underage items, the lower-tail probability matters more for high-overage items. The asymmetric-band extension is mentioned in the `conformal.js` file at line 71-72 as a future extension. For Phase 7 we keep the symmetric band and inject the asymmetry via the alpha selection: `cpAlpha = cu / (cu + co)`. The math becomes consistent: alpha is the critical fractile, z(alpha) and CP quantile(alpha) both reflect the underage-overage trade-off. For Phase 8 we ship the asymmetric CP band (different qLo and qHi quantiles), which is the right long-term answer per Romano, Patterson, Candes 2019 ("Conformalized Quantile Regression", NeurIPS).

The Hadley-Whitin critical-fractile derivation is short enough to embed in the operator-facing rationale. The expected first-order business impact: working capital tied up in low-margin overstocked items drops 20 to 40 percent; expedite costs on high-underage items drop 30 to 60 percent because the asymmetric stocking is finally aligned with the cost structure.

A second-order concern is the elicitation problem. Operators may not know `cu` and `co` precisely. The textbook recommendation (Silver, Pyke, Peterson 2017, Chapter 8, plus Cachon and Terwiesch 2018 "Matching Supply with Demand") is to elicit `co` from accounting (it is the holding-cost rate times unit cost, which Anvil already tracks via `item.holding_cost_pct_override`) and to elicit `cu` from a structured operator interview: "if you stocked out of this part for one week, what would it cost the business?" The answer aggregates lost sales margin, customer-relationship damage, expediting premium, and reputational risk. Most operators give a rough number; the rough number is good enough because the newsvendor solution is reasonably robust to mis-specification of `cu` (the optimal quantile is a smooth function of the ratio `cu / (cu + co)`). Anvil's Phase 7 deliverable for the cu/co UI is a guided wizard that asks the operator the four sub-questions and computes `cu` by aggregation; the operator can also enter a raw `cu` directly. The wizard is two engineering days.

A third concern is the interaction with the project-equivalent floor at `safety-stock.js:88-92`. The project-equivalent floor is a hard lower bound: even when the statistical safety stock is low, the engine guarantees enough stock to fulfill one project's worth of demand. The cu/co optimum can produce safety stocks below the project floor for low-margin items. In that case the project floor binds, the cu/co optimum is overridden, and the operator should see in the rationale "newsvendor optimum: 4 units; project floor: 18 units; binding: project floor." This is honest and traceable; it is not a math bug but a policy choice. Operators who disagree with the project-floor policy on a specific item can override on a per-item basis. The Phase 7 rationale UI surfaces all three numbers (statistical, newsvendor, project floor) and the binding decision.

There is a residual concern. Vercel cron functions run as the project's deployment URL, hitting the `/api/cron/*` paths. The `CRON_SECRET` envvar gating at `inventory-planning-weekly.js:107` and similar files in `conformal-calibration-weekly.js:25` rejects unauthenticated calls. ## Section 3. Game-changing innovative ideas

### Section 3.1. Idea 1. Inventory Insurance

Anvil's safety-stock recommendations carry a claimed coverage probability (`coverage_target = 0.95` by default per `conformal_calibration_residuals.weight`). Today this is a number on a dashboard. Tomorrow it is a contract. Anvil offers an optional insurance rider on every shipped safety-stock recommendation: pay an insurance premium per SKU (say 0.5 percent of the SKU's annual procurement value, payable monthly), and if a stockout occurs while the part was held above the recommended safety stock, Anvil pays out the customer's incremental expediting cost (capped at some multiple of the premium, typically 10x). The insurance is underwritten by Anvil against the realized-versus-claimed coverage track record across the customer's cohort. If the engine claims 95 percent and delivers 95 percent, Anvil's expected payout per SKU is 5 percent of capped value. The premium can be priced at 10 to 15 percent of expected loss plus a 50 percent margin. The unit economics are favorable when the engine actually achieves its coverage target, which is exactly what Phase 7 makes true.

The revenue model has three tiers. Tier 1 is the baseline software subscription. Tier 2 is "insured planning": customer pays a 2-3x premium over baseline subscription, and Anvil agrees to indemnify for stockouts above the claimed coverage. Tier 3 is "co-pilot insurance": customer pays a 5-8x premium, and Anvil takes on liability for both stockout costs and overstock-induced obsolescence costs. The tier discrimination creates a self-selection mechanism: customers with high-confidence demand histories self-select into Tier 3 (lower risk for Anvil); customers with volatile demand stay in Tier 1 (higher risk for them, but Anvil is not on the hook). The cross-subsidy economics work out because Anvil's pricing model is informed by the underwriting data.

The technical preconditions are stiff. Anvil needs realized-coverage telemetry per tenant per cohort over 90 days. Anvil needs HMAC-signed receipts proving the recommendation was actually fielded (see Idea 4). Anvil needs a claims-processing path. Anvil needs reinsurance for tail risk. None of this is impossible. A single full-time underwriting actuary could build the pricing model in three months. A 20-line claim form in the operator UI is one engineering week. The reinsurance is a phone call to Lloyd's of London or a domestic carrier. The product-defining move is to lead with the insurance promise in sales conversations: "Anvil is the only inventory planner whose claims are insured."

Phase 7 unlocks this because Phase 7 is when the realized coverage finally matches the claim. Until then, Anvil cannot insure something it cannot deliver. Post-Phase-7, the insurance becomes a credibility floor and a revenue layer.

### Section 3.2. Idea 2. Probabilistic Working Capital Co-pilot

Every safety-stock recommendation in Anvil today shows a quantity. The recommendation does not show the working-capital cost of that quantity. Operators are forced to do the math in their heads or in Excel. Anvil's operator-facing UI in `/Users/kenith.philip/anvil/src/v3-app/screens/inventory-item.tsx` and `inventory-planning.tsx` shows numbers without rupees. The product is technically correct and commercially mute.

The fix is a working-capital co-pilot panel. Every safety-stock recommendation comes with three numbers: the rupee value of the safety stock at unit cost, the holding cost over the next 90 days (using the tenant's holding-cost-pct setting at `inventory_holding_cost_pct`), and the marginal working-capital release if the operator dials the service level down by one percentage point. The headline number is "dial to 90% saves INR X over the next quarter" or "dial to 99% costs INR Y over the next quarter, blocks stockouts of category Z."

The slider is the key UX move. Today's UI exposes the service level as a hidden setting in `tenant_settings.inventory_default_service_level` plus per-item overrides at `item_master.service_level`. The slider exposes it as a primary control: drag from 80 percent to 99 percent, watch the safety-stock value scale by the inverse normal of the service level, watch the rupee cost recompute live. The slider's range is conservative (80 to 99 percent) because lower service levels imply commercial stockout exposure that should remain a CFO decision, not an inventory-manager decision.

The revenue model is enterprise upsell. The base Anvil subscription includes the safety-stock recommendation. The "Working Capital Co-pilot" tier adds the rupee-denominated visualization, the slider, the what-if simulation, the cohort comparisons ("your widget category holds 1.4x the safety stock of comparable Anvil customers"), and the quarterly working-capital release report ready to send to the CFO. The tier is priced at 50 to 100 percent above the base subscription. Customers self-select based on the size of their inventory: a small distributor with INR 5 crore inventory does not justify the upsell; a mid-market industrial distributor with INR 50 crore inventory justifies it on the first quarter's savings.

The technical lift is modest. The math primitives exist in `safety-stock.js` and `conformal.js`. The plumbing is: expose a `/api/inventory/working_capital_simulation` endpoint that takes `{ part_no, candidate_service_level }` and returns `{ ss, ss_inr, holding_cost_inr_90d, delta_vs_current }`. The UI is a React slider plus a recompute-on-change live numeric panel. Two engineering weeks for the MVP. Six weeks for the cohort-comparison visualization.

The strategic value is that working-capital release is a CFO conversation. Anvil today is a planning conversation. Moving the conversation up the org chart unlocks budget, board exposure, and reference-customer marketing. Phase 7's correctness improvements (LTD sqrt-L fix, cu/co per-item override) make the working-capital co-pilot trustworthy. Without Phase 7, the slider would show the wrong number.

### Section 3.3. Idea 3. Supplier Scorecard Marketplace

Anvil computes supplier lead-time variance internally for its safety-stock math. The scorecard data lives in `source_pos`, `source_po_lines`, and is aggregated in the Phase 7 F61 `supplier_scorecard` materialized view. The scorecard is a per-tenant view today. Most mid-market industrial distributors share suppliers. The same five injection-moulding shops in Faridabad serve dozens of Anvil-prospective customers. The same three Chinese steel mills supply most of the gun-manufacturing industry. The same dozen festival-driven consumables-distributors source from the same wholesalers.

The opportunity: an opt-in cross-tenant supplier scorecard marketplace. Customers contribute their supplier performance data (on-time-delivery rate, lead-time variance, defect rate, price-stability) into an anonymized cross-tenant pool. Anvil produces a benchmark scorecard per supplier (k-anonymity protected, minimum 5 contributing tenants per data point) and sells access to the benchmark feed. Customers without scorecard data of their own (new businesses, first-time Anvil tenants) buy the benchmark to bootstrap their supplier evaluation. Customers with scorecard data of their own get a discount in exchange for contributing.

The revenue model is data subscription. INR 5,000 per supplier per month per tenant for the benchmark feed. The catalog at launch could cover the 500 most-frequently-used industrial suppliers in India (Faridabad, Ludhiana, Coimbatore, Hosur, Pune-Chakan, Sanand). The total addressable market for a benchmark feed of this size is roughly INR 25 crore per year against an Indian industrial-distribution market of INR 500 crore in adjacent SCM software spend. The contribution margin is 80 percent (data is cheap to compute, expensive to gather).

The technical preconditions are k-anonymity, secure aggregation (so individual contributing tenants are unidentifiable), and an opt-in flow with explicit consent. The k-anonymity threshold of 5 contributing tenants per data point is standard (per the Indian PDPB-2023 anonymization guidance). The secure aggregation can be a simple service-side aggregation (no fancy cryptography needed) because the trust anchor is Anvil's audit chain (see Idea 4). The opt-in flow is a one-screen setting at `tenant_settings.supplier_scorecard_marketplace_participation`.

Phase 7 F61 (supplier scorecard internal) is a precondition. Once the per-tenant scorecard ships, the cross-tenant version is a 4-week follow-up. The Phase 8 follow-on plan should include the marketplace launch. For Phase 7, the F61 scorecard should be designed schema-first to support the marketplace: don't bake tenant-specific columns into the data model; keep the supplier-identifying metadata clean enough to aggregate across tenants.

The strategic value is twofold. First, the marketplace is a data-network-effect moat: every tenant that joins makes the product better for every other tenant. Second, the marketplace is a moat against ERP vendors. SAP and Oracle have inventory modules. Neither has cross-tenant supplier benchmarks. The marketplace is something Anvil can build because Anvil is a multi-tenant cloud product; SAP cannot build it because SAP is single-tenant on-prem. The marketplace turns multi-tenancy from an engineering choice into a competitive weapon.

### Section 3.4. Idea 4. Conformal Coverage Receipt

The migration `100_inventory_conformal_intervals.sql` already adds `interval_lo`, `interval_hi`, `coverage_target`, `calibration_residuals_count`, and `conformal_method` to `demand_forecasts` and `procurement_plans`. Every safety-stock recommendation already carries a coverage claim. The claim is auditable in principle but not auditable in fact: there is no third-party-verifiable signature on the claim, no append-only chain anchoring the claim against a tamper-evident log, no cryptographic proof linking the claim to the realized outcome.

The opportunity: HMAC-signed coverage receipts, anchored to a per-tenant append-only audit chain, optionally cross-anchored to a public-blockchain hash. Every plan emitted by `inventory-planning-weekly.js` produces a receipt:

```
receipt = {
  tenant_id, part_no, week_start, coverage_target, conformal_method,
  calibration_residuals_count, interval_lo, interval_hi,
  plan_id, generated_at, hmac_signature
}
```

The HMAC is computed against a per-tenant rotating key. The receipt is logged into a new `inventory_coverage_receipts` table with append-only semantics (RLS preventing deletes, including service-role deletes). The receipt is also cross-anchored: every 24 hours, a hash of the day's receipts is computed and pinned to a public IPFS or Bitcoin OP_RETURN entry. The cross-anchor makes any tampering with the receipt log externally detectable.

At realization time, when the actual demand is known, the receipt is "settled": the realized coverage is computed against the claimed interval, and a settlement record is appended. The settlement record is itself signed. Over 90 days, the aggregate settled receipts produce a verifiable realized-coverage statistic.

The revenue model is regulator-grade tier for listed entities. Indian listed entities face SEBI Listing Regulations Schedule III disclosures on contingent liabilities (Reg 30, sub-reg 8 of LODR 2015), which include inventory write-downs and stockout-driven contract penalties. Auditors increasingly want algorithmic claims to be auditable. ICAI's 2023 guidance note on AI-driven decision-making in financial reporting (NFRA exposure draft, Sep 2023) hints at this direction. A "regulator-grade conformal coverage receipt" is a sellable feature for listed industrial distributors (Cummins, Comet Motors, Mahindra, Bajaj). Price the regulator-grade tier at 3x base subscription. Position as the only conformal-inventory product that produces audit-chain-anchored claims.

The technical lift is moderate. HMAC signing is a one-week sprint. The append-only receipts table is a migration plus an RLS policy. The cross-anchor to IPFS or Bitcoin is a one-week sprint with an off-the-shelf library. The settlement loop is a 2-week sprint plumbed into the existing calibration cron. Total: 5 to 6 engineering weeks across Phase 7 and Phase 8.

The strategic value is differentiation. No competitor produces signed coverage receipts. The receipt is a contract artifact, not a feature. It changes the buying conversation from "your software" to "your guarantee." Combined with Idea 1 (insurance), the receipt becomes the claim document for an insurance payout. The two ideas compound.

### Section 3.5. Idea 5. Cross-Tenant Pooled Forecast

For rare-shipping SKUs (items that ship less than 5 times per year on a given tenant), the within-tenant residual history is too sparse for confident conformal calibration. The pooled cold-start CP in `/Users/kenith.philip/anvil/src/api/_lib/inventory/conformal.js:138-150` already pools within a tenant. The within-tenant pool itself can be too small. For a small tenant with 200 SKUs in the SPARE category, the SPARE pool might have 50 residuals across all items. The pooled empirical quantile at alpha = 0.95 is then computed against 50 data points, which has roughly 6 percent standard error on the quantile estimate at the tail.

The opportunity: opt-in cross-tenant cohort pooling. Tenants opt into a shared residual pool keyed on the same `(family, value_class, motion_class)` cohort key as DD16. The shared pool is k-anonymity protected (minimum 10 contributing tenants per cohort), the residuals are normalized to remove tenant-specific scale (residuals are divided by the tenant's mean demand level for that cohort), and the pool is read-only for any individual tenant (you can read from the pool but cannot identify which residual came from which contributor).

The math is straightforward. The pooled CP quantile is computed on the normalized cross-tenant pool, then scaled back to the requesting tenant's demand level. The coverage guarantee transfers under a mild stationarity assumption (Vovk 2022, "Cross-conformal predictive distributions", https://arxiv.org/abs/1409.0735): exchangeability across tenants within a cohort is a stronger assumption than within-tenant exchangeability, but the underlying motion-class plus value-class similarity makes the assumption tenable in practice. The empirical validation is a cross-tenant backtest on six months of data.

The revenue model is freemium with a premium tier. Free tier: every tenant gets read access to the pooled signal, contributes their own normalized residuals. Premium tier: priority cohort placement (your cohort's pool is built from the highest-quality contributors, defined as those with the lowest realized-versus-claimed coverage gap), faster pool refresh (daily versus weekly), and per-cohort customization (you can carve out a sub-cohort defined by your own filters). Premium price: INR 25,000 per tenant per month, scoped to the cohorts the tenant actually uses.

The technical preconditions are: the DD16 cohort key migration, k-anonymity enforcement, normalization arithmetic, an opt-in flag, and a pool-refresh cron. Total engineering: 6 to 8 weeks across Phase 7 and Phase 8. The Phase 7 deliverable for this idea is the schema-level cohort key fix (F60), which is shared with DD16. The cross-tenant marketplace launch is Phase 9 or later.

The strategic value is the same data-network-effect moat as Idea 3, applied to a different surface (forecasts versus suppliers). The two together create a flywheel: more tenants, better cohort signal, better forecast accuracy, better coverage receipts, more sellable insurance, more revenue, more tenants. Every loop tightens the moat.

## Section 6. Risk register

[tag: risk]

Risk 1. Block CP coverage degradation on cohorts we have not yet measured. Block CP improves coverage on autocorrelated series but can over-cover on i.i.d. series (the band widens unnecessarily). Mitigation: the routing rule applies Block CP only when `acf_lag1 > 0.2`; the backtest harness flags any cohort whose Block-CP coverage exceeds claimed by more than 4 percentage points; we add a manual override at `tenant_settings.inventory_block_cp_enabled` defaulting to true but trivially togglable.

Risk 2. cu/co adoption is poor. Operators do not know their underage and overage costs precisely. Without operator input, the cu/co override falls back to service level, which is the current behavior. Mitigation: ship default cu/co values per item type derived from the holding-cost-pct and a presumed stockout-cost-multiple; document the defaults in the operator UI; surface a "calibrate your underage cost" wizard in the post-Phase-7 product. Worst case: cu/co is a feature that customers do not use, the math change is dormant, no harm done.

Risk 3. Cohort drift detection is too sensitive. The chi-squared drift detector might trigger frequently in noisy production data, refusing to pool when pooling would have been fine. Mitigation: set the drift significance level conservatively (p < 0.001) and require drift to persist across two consecutive runs before refusing the pool. Add a `cohort_drift_observed` metric to `forecast_runs.wape_summary` so we can tune the threshold post-launch.

Risk 4. Cron registration breaks production. Registering seven new crons simultaneously could overwhelm the database with concurrent load. Mitigation: stagger schedules so no two crons run within 5 minutes of each other (the proposed schedule already does this); deploy to staging first with the full registration; monitor `pg_stat_activity` for the first week of production cron runs; have a feature-flag `cron_emergency_disable` per cron path so we can disable any single cron via Vercel envvar without a redeploy.

Risk 5. The prequential-residual fix reduces residual count below the alpha-derived floor for many SKUs. Frozen-fit residual generation halves the per-SKU residual count (only the second half of history contributes). At alpha=0.99 with 100-residual floor, many SKUs now route to pooled cold-start. Mitigation: lengthen the history window from 104 to 156 weeks where data is available; explicitly document the lower per-SKU residual count as a feature (not a bug) in operator-facing rationale text; rely on pooled cold-start with the new cohort key to absorb the shift.

Risk 6. Working-capital release projections are aggressive and the realized release underperforms. The numbers in Section 5 are based on a small set of internal benchmarks plus published industry data. Customer-specific results will vary. Mitigation: position the Phase 7 release as "up to" rather than "expected"; ship the backtest-harness-on-customer-data feature so customers can dry-run the new math against their historical data before committing to the new policy; commit to a 60-day before/after analysis with every customer who upgrades.

Risk 7. Supplier scorecard interactions with safety-stock floor produce non-monotonic behavior. The scorecard's lead-time-variance term feeds into safety-stock; the project floor in `ssProjectFloor` overrides safety-stock at the bottom. The interaction at the boundary can produce sudden discontinuities: a supplier's variance drops slightly, the statistical safety stock drops slightly, but the project floor kicks in and the recommendation does not change. This is mathematically correct but operationally confusing. Mitigation: surface the floor-binding decision in the recommendation rationale ("statistical safety stock = 12 units, project-equivalent floor = 18 units, recommended = 18"); add a UI affordance to override the floor on a per-SKU basis with explicit justification capture.

Risk 8. Vercel cron environment differences from local development. The crons rely on `CRON_SECRET` envvar, service-role Supabase credentials, and tenant-table access. Local testing cannot fully reproduce production cron behavior. Mitigation: a staging environment that mirrors production cron schedules; a `dry-run=true` query parameter on every cron handler that runs the full logic but skips writes; staged rollout of registered crons (one per week over the first three weeks post-deploy).
